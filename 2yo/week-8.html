Gross Motor Pod
Fine Motor: Building Towers and Stacking
FMS: Bilateral Coordination and Precision
▾
Why This Matters

Tower building requires bilateral hand coordination, visual-motor integration, and precision grip. The group tower introduces the social dimension of fine motor play: waiting for someone else to place their block.

🏃 FMS Technique Cues (Munch & Move)
Two hands working together
Place gently — don't drop
Eyes on the top of the tower
Steady the base with one hand
Count each block as it goes on
Activities
  • Stack 4–6 (EYLF 4): Individual tower building — aim for a new personal best.
  • Group tower (EYLF 1): One block at a time, taking turns. How high can the group go?
  • Knock + rebuild (EYLF 4): The knocking is as important as the building.

VariationBuild on different surfaces — develops problem-solving about stability.

👪 Parent Coaching Focus: Scaffold and Step Back
🏠 Try This at Home

Build towers at home with anything that stacks — tin cans, books, blocks. Count out loud.

🫀 Body Awareness This Week

When your child gets frustrated and pushes away from an activity, that's a signal — tired, overwhelmed, or done. Naming it: "Your body is telling you it needs a break" teaches them to recognise and trust that signal themselves.

Equipment
Wooden or foam blocks
🗣️
Communication Pod
Using Pronouns
🦉 OWL
▾
Why This Matters

Pronouns require the child to understand that the same person is referred to differently depending on who is speaking — a complex perspective-taking task embedded in grammar.

🦉 OWL Reminder — Observe, Wait, Listen

OWL — Listen this week: hear whether your child is starting to use 'me', 'mine', or 'I'. Reflect these back when you hear them.

Activities
  • Role-play 'I eat/you eat' (EYLF 5): At snack, model 'I'm eating. You're eating.'
  • Story he/she (EYLF 5): Point to characters in books and use he/she consistently.
  • Imitation 'me/you' (EYLF 5): Touch your chest and say 'me,' touch child's chest and say 'you.'

VariationUse photos of family members.

👪 Parent Coaching Focus: Describe and Narrate
🏠 Try This at Home

Use 'I' and 'you' explicitly at meals. Don't correct when they confuse them — just model correctly.

📚 Book of the Week — Hanen Book Nook

I Love You, Blue Kangaroo — Emma Chichester Clark

Natural pronoun use (I, you, he, she) in a relational emotional context — matching the pronouns theme. The warmth of the story connects to the SE pod this week. Pause at pronoun moments and echo them back warmly.

Cognitive Pod
Sequencing
EF: WM (hold sequence in mind)
▾
Why This Matters

Harvard (2014): imitation games that require remembering and copying a sequence practice attention, working memory, and self-control simultaneously.

🧠 Executive Function Skill This Week

Working Memory (hold sequence in mind) + Inhibitory Control (complete each step before rushing)

Activities
  • Block tower in steps (EYLF 4): Adult builds one block at a time with a pause — child copies.
  • Ring stack in order (EYLF 4): Stack rings largest to smallest.
  • Clap sequence (EYLF 5): 2–3 clap pattern for child to copy. Add complexity when mastered.

Variation3-photo sequence cards (eat, drink, wash) — put them in order.

👪 Parent Coaching Focus: Follow Child's Lead
🏠 Try This at Home

Draw 3 pictures of the bedtime routine. Let your child put them in order before bed.

Equipment
Unit blocksStacking ringsSequence picture cards

Social-Emotional Pod
Turn-Taking — Structured Games
CASEL: Self-Management + Relationship Skills
▾
Why This Matters

Turn-taking is the social expression of inhibitory control. At 2 years, successful turn-taking requires short waits and concrete signals. The wait itself is the developmental target.

Activities
  • Roll ball (EYLF 1): Back-and-forth. Name clearly: 'my turn... your turn.' Keep waits very short.
  • Pass drum (EYLF 1): Pass around a circle. Name who is holding it.
  • Peek-a-boo turns (EYLF 5): Take turns hiding and revealing.

VariationUse a visual 'turn token' — a coloured card showing whose turn it is.

🌱 Development Note (Erikson)

Social competence begins with the ability to delay action for another person.

👪 Parent Coaching Focus: Narrate the Wait

Parents learn that verbalising the wait is more helpful than redirecting when a child struggles.

🏠 Try This at Home

Roll a ball back and forth. Count the turns out loud. Three or four turns in a row is genuinely impressive.

Equipment
Soft ballDrumTurn token card

Self-Help Pod
Self-Feeding — Spoon and Finger Foods
ADL: Feeding
▾
Why This Matters

By 2 years, most children can feed themselves with their fingers effectively. The barrier to independence is almost always parental anxiety about mess, not child capacity.

Activities
  • Finger foods (EYLF 3): Appropriate finger foods served without assistance.
  • Small spoon practice (EYLF 3): Loaded spoon handed to child — they bring it to mouth.
  • Celebrate feeding self (EYLF 3): 'You fed yourself! You got it all in!'

VariationFork practice with soft fruit — slightly easier than spoon for children who struggle with rotation.

⚠️ Common Parent Trap

Parents often alternate between doing it all and expecting full independence. The scaffold sequence should stay consistent — same level of support every meal.

🌱 Development Note (Erikson)

Autonomy: the child who feeds themselves — however messily — is experiencing bodily agency.

👪 Parent Coaching Focus: Support Then Release
🏠 Try This at Home

One meal a day where your child does all the feeding themselves. Sit beside them, narrate, celebrate.

Equipment
Appropriate finger foodsSmall spoonsBibs and floor mat
