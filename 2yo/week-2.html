Week 2

Week 2 expands vocabulary through action words and naming, builds gross motor confidence with climbing and step work, and introduces cause-and-effect problem-solving. The social-emotional focus is on separation and transitions, with self-help building on drinking independence.

Gross Motor Pod
Stability: Climbing + Step Work
FMS: Climbing
▾
Why This Matters

Step climbing is a key stability milestone, building single-leg balance, weight transfer, and spatial awareness. Alternating feet on stairs typically emerges between 24–36 months. This week consolidates climbing with more specific step-focus.

🏃 FMS Technique Cues (Munch & Move)
One hand on rail for support
Lead foot steps up first
Bring feet together on each step before continuing
Eyes look ahead not down
Knees bend on descent
Activities
  • Climbing frame (EYLF 3): Continue from Week 1 with increased expectation of independence on familiar equipment.
  • Step climbing (EYLF 3): Low steps with handrail. Encourage marking-time pattern before expecting alternating feet.
  • Crawl + climb course (EYLF 3): Combine crawling through tunnels with climbing over low obstacles.
  • Body awareness (EYLF 3): Name body parts in movement — 'lift your knee,' 'reach your arm up.'

VariationTape footprints on low steps to help children understand where to place their feet.

👪 Parent Coaching Focus: Scaffold and Step Back
🏠 Try This at Home

Find three steps at home or the park. Practise going up and down slowly, narrating each step. Hold her hand lightly — the goal is she feels the movement herself.

🫀 Body Awareness This Week

When your child hesitates at the top of a step, their body is giving them real safety information. That pause deserves respect, not hurrying. Try: "Your body is telling you something — what is it saying?"

Low climbing frameSteps with handrailTunnel or crawl space

Vocabulary Expansion — Action Words and Naming
🦉 OWL
▾
Why This Matters

Action words (verbs) are the engine of sentence building. Children with strong verb vocabulary move faster into multi-word combinations. The mystery bag provides high-interest motivation for labelling.

🦉 OWL Reminder — Observe, Wait, Listen

OWL — this week focus on the Wait. When your child reaches for something, count silently to five before you label it. You might be surprised what they say first.

Activities
  • Name 5 toys (EYLF 5): Hold up toys one at a time and wait — don't label until after the expectant pause.
  • Mystery bag (EYLF 5): Child feels an object and describes it before seeing it. 'What is it? Soft? Big?'
  • Outdoor naming walk (EYLF 2): Walk around naming objects, animals, plants. Child leads; adult follows and labels.

VariationPhotograph objects on the naming walk and make a simple picture book.

👪 Parent Coaching Focus: Observe and Follow
🏠 Try This at Home

Try the mystery bag at home — put a familiar object in a pillowcase and let your child feel and describe it before looking.

📚 Book of the Week — Hanen Book Nook

From Head to Toe — Eric Carle

Every page features an animal performing an action — 'I can do it!' Pause after each animal and wait for your child to name the action or copy the movement. Action words are the communication theme this week.

Problem-Solving — Pull String, Puzzle Knob, Nesting Cups
EF: WM (track where toy went)
▾
Why This Matters

Cause-and-effect problem-solving builds the 'if I do X, Y happens' mental model. Harvard (2014): these activities require children to hold the goal in mind and try new approaches when the first attempt fails — practising both working memory and inhibitory control.

🧠 Executive Function Skill This Week

Working Memory (track where toy went) + Inhibitory Control (try differently when first attempt fails)

Activities
  • Pull string for toy (EYLF 4): Toy on a string beyond reach. Child must pull string to get it.
  • Puzzle knob (EYLF 4): Single-piece lift-out puzzles with large knobs. Focus on fitting back in.
  • Nesting cups (EYLF 4): Cups of varying sizes to nest and stack. Let the child discover size relationships.

VariationLazy Susan turntable to spin a hidden object — adds working memory challenge.

👪 Parent Coaching Focus: Follow Child's Lead
🏠 Try This at Home

Nesting cups in the bath — fill, pour, stack. Add a funnel for extra challenge.

Equipment
Pull-string toySingle-piece puzzlesNesting cups (5–8)

Social-Emotional Pod
Separation and Transitions
CASEL: Self-Management
▾
Why This Matters

Separation distress at transitions is normal and healthy at 2 years — it signals secure attachment. Gottman (1997): labelling the emotion ('you feel sad when mummy goes') is more effective than reassurance because it validates the experience rather than dismissing it.

Activities
  • Short goodbye (EYLF 1): Consistent, brief goodbye ritual. Prolonged goodbyes increase distress.
  • Comfort toy (EYLF 3): Allow children to bring a familiar object from home — a co-regulation tool.
  • Transition song (EYLF 5): Consistent song to signal transitions. Predictability reduces resistance.

VariationMake a 'goodbye book' — photos of parent and child for the child to look at during the session.

🎯 Facilitator Coaching Cues

When a parent lingers after goodbye: "The kindest thing is often a warm, brief goodbye — the longer it goes, the harder it gets for both of them."

When a child is distressed: "She's telling us she knows you're going — that's secure attachment. Let's name it: 'You feel sad. Daddy will come back.'"

🌱 Development Note (Erikson)

Autonomy: the child who can manage a transition with a comfort object and a named emotion is practising self-regulation.

👪 Parent Coaching Focus: Name It to Tame It

Parents learn that naming the child's emotion is more helpful than rushing to fix or dismiss it.

🏠 Try This at Home

Practise a goodbye routine at home — same words, same hug, same wave. Consistency makes it easier each time.

Equipment
Comfort toys from homeTransition songGoodbye book (photos)

Drinking — Open Cup and Pouring
ADL: Feeding
▾
Why This Matters

Moving from a sippy cup to an open cup requires fine motor control and proprioceptive awareness. Introducing pouring at this stage is ambitious but appropriate with a small pitcher into a large cup.

Activities
  • Practice open cup (EYLF 3): Small open cups with a small amount of liquid. Stabilise the table, not the cup.
  • Tea party pouring (EYLF 3): Small pitcher with water into cups. Start with minimal amount.
  • Self-serve snack (EYLF 3): Child carries their own snack from the table to their seat.

VariationUse a small jug with a lip — easier to pour accurately.

⚠️ Common Parent Trap

Parents often grab the cup when spilling is about to happen. Key message: place a small towel under the cup and let the spill happen. The child learns more from the spill than from the prevention.

🌱 Development Note (Erikson)

Autonomy: pouring their own drink is one of the earliest experiences of 'I did that myself' in a mealtime context.

👪 Parent Coaching Focus: Support Then Release
🏠 Try This at Home

Let your child pour their own drink at one meal a day — use a small amount in a small pitcher. Put a towel down and step back.
