Week 5

Gross Motor Pod
Locomotion: Running with Control
FMS: Running
▾
Why This Matters

Controlled running — starting, stopping, changing direction — underpins playground safety and sport participation. The stop/start game directly builds inhibitory control alongside the physical skill.

🏃 FMS Technique Cues (Munch & Move)
Eyes straight ahead
Swing your arms
Knees up
Run to the cone — stop at the line
Fast feet, then freeze
Activities
  • Follow-the-leader (EYLF 3): Adult leads a running path with turns, stops, and speeds.
  • Stop/start game (EYLF 3): Music or drum signal. Run when it plays, freeze when it stops.
  • Run chasing bubbles (EYLF 3): Directional running with a natural target.

VariationAdd obstacle cones to weave between.

👪 Parent Coaching Focus: Scaffold and Step Back
🏠 Try This at Home

Play freeze dance at home — any music works. The stopping is the skill.

🫀 Body Awareness This Week

After running, try: "Your heart is beating fast — can you feel it? Put your hand here." That's your body talking to you. Cardiac interoception is one of the earliest body signals children can be helped to notice.

Communication Pod
Labelling Body Parts
🦉 OWL
▾
Why This Matters

Body part vocabulary is foundational for self-expression and medical communication. Songs that pair word and action build vocabulary and motor-language integration.

🦉 OWL Reminder — Observe, Wait, Listen

OWL — Observe: when your child touches their head, say 'head!' Connect the label to their action.

Activities
  • 'Head, Shoulders, Knees and Toes' (EYLF 5): Slow version first — point to each part.
  • Mirror body parts (EYLF 3): Child touches own face in mirror as adult names parts.
  • Picture matching (EYLF 4): Match body part cards to a picture of a person.

VariationDraw around the child's body on paper and label the parts.

👪 Parent Coaching Focus: Describe and Narrate
🏠 Try This at Home

Sing 'Head, Shoulders' in the bath while washing — touch each part as you name it.

Cognitive Pod
Sorting by Colour
EF: CF (group by one rule)
▾
Why This Matters

Harvard (2014): sorting activities are foundational for cognitive flexibility and can be extended by changing the rule — challenging children to inhibit the old rule and apply the new one.

🧠 Executive Function Skill This Week

Cognitive Flexibility (group by one rule) + Working Memory (hold the sorting rule in mind)

Activities
  • Colour sort toys (EYLF 4): Two containers, two colours.
  • Animals vs cars (EYLF 4): Two categories, mixed objects.
  • Food play grouping (EYLF 5): Sort play food into 'fruit' and 'other.'

VariationAfter sorting by colour, immediately ask 'now let's sort by size' — the cognitive flexibility challenge.

👪 Parent Coaching Focus: Follow Child's Lead
🏠 Try This at Home

Sort laundry together — colours vs whites. Real-world categorisation is the best context.

Equipment
Sorting containersMixed colour toysPlay food set

Social-Emotional Pod
Parallel Play — Consolidation
CASEL: Relationship Skills
▾
Why This Matters

By Week 5 children should be settling into parallel play with increasing comfort. Watch for the shift toward associative play — children referencing each other's activities — and narrate it when it happens.

Activities
  • Blocks side-by-side (EYLF 2): Same materials, own space. Adult narrates both children's activity.
  • Bubble play (EYLF 1): Shared experience that often prompts spontaneous peer interaction.
  • Sandpit with peers (EYLF 2): Open-ended, low-pressure shared space.

VariationObserve for and narrate moments of associative play: 'she's watching what you're doing!'

🌱 Development Note (Erikson)

Relationship skills begin with tolerance of proximity.

👪 Parent Coaching Focus: Warm Attention — Narrate the Group

Parents learn to narrate the social environment — not just their own child's activity.

🏠 Try This at Home

Arrange a playdate with one other child. Don't organise activities — just provide the same materials and watch.

Self-Help Pod
Shoes and Socks
ADL: Dressing
▾
Why This Matters

Foot dressing is harder than arm dressing because children cannot see their feet as clearly. Sock-off is the appropriate starting point — undressing always precedes dressing.

Activities
  • Pull socks off (EYLF 3): Loose sock over the toes — child pulls it off.
  • Push feet into shoes (EYLF 3): Velcro shoes placed open on floor — child pushes foot in.
  • Celebrate independence (EYLF 3): Name what happened: 'You pulled your sock off all by yourself!'

VariationSlip-on shoes before velcro — the pushing motion is simpler.

⚠️ Common Parent Trap

Parents put on shoes for children because shoes are the last step before going out — when everyone is in a hurry. Suggest practising at a non-urgent time first.

🌱 Development Note (Erikson)

Autonomy: pulling off their own socks is often a child's first independent dressing act.

👪 Parent Coaching Focus: Support Then Release
🏠 Try This at Home

Let your child take their own shoes and socks off before bath every night this week.

Equipment
