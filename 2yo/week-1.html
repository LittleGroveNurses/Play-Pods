Week 1

Week 1 introduces 2-year-olds to simple sentence building, gross motor exploration through climbing and running, and basic problem-solving with everyday objects. Social-emotional play focuses on parallel play with peers and learning to take turns. Self-help activities encourage greater independence at mealtimes and during dressing.

Gross Motor Pod
Locomotion: Running + Stability: Climbing
FMS: Running
▾
Why This Matters

At 2 years, locomotion and stability are being built simultaneously. Running with control and climbing (requiring upper body strength, bilateral coordination, and spatial judgement) are foundational to later playground confidence and physical safety.

🏃 FMS Technique Cues (Munch & Move)
Eyes looking straight ahead
Swing your arms
Knees up when running
Choose own route up — don't direct the climb
Feet placed deliberately, not rushed
Activities
  • Climbing (EYLF 3): Small frames, steps, or playground equipment. Encourage children to choose their own route up and down.
  • Running games (EYLF 3): Stop-and-go running in open space — 'Red Light, Green Light' style. Builds locomotion skill and impulse control together.
  • Ball skills (EYLF 3): Kick, throw, and chase soft balls. Unstructured — the goal is exploratory contact, not accuracy.
  • Fine motor — stacking and pegboards (EYLF 4): 6–8 blocks and pegboard play builds hand-eye coordination and pincer grip.

VariationCreate a mini obstacle course combining climbing, crawling, and running.

🪜 Scaffolding This Activity

Climbing: (1) Adult climbs alongside narrating each step. (2) Adult spots from below — hands ready but not touching. (3) Adult stands at base, available. (4) Child climbs independently.

Running: Start with short distances and exaggerated 'stop' cues. Build the brake before the accelerator.

Ball skills: Any foot-to-ball contact or deliberate throw is a success. Celebrate contact, not direction.

🎯 Facilitator Coaching Cues

When a parent lifts the child onto the frame: "Let's put him at the bottom and see if he can work out how to get up — he might surprise you."

When a child runs but can't stop: "She's getting the running — the stopping comes next. Try 'freeze!' with a big pose yourself and see if she copies it."

When a parent corrects the ball kick: "At this age any kick is a great kick — let's cheer whatever he does. Technique comes much later."

👪 Parent Coaching Focus: Scaffold and Step Back

Parents learn to set up the physical environment then step back and let the child problem-solve. Watch for parents who lift rather than let the child work it out, or who roll the ball directly to the child's foot rather than placing and waiting.

🏠 Try This at Home

Set up a simple obstacle course at home — a cushion to climb over, a line of tape to run along, a ball to kick into a basket. Let your child lead. Narrate: 'You climbed over! Now running! Big kick!'

Equipment
Small climbing frame or stepsSoft ballsBlocks (6–8 stackable)Pegboards and pegs

Communication Pod
Two- to Three-Word Phrases
🦉 OWL
▾
Why This Matters

Two- to three-word phrases mark the transition from labelling to expressing relationships and intentions. Embedding phrase-building into play and routines — rather than drilling vocabulary — produces faster and more durable gains.

🦉 OWL Reminder — Observe, Wait, Listen

OWL — Observe, Wait, Listen: Before modelling a phrase this week, try watching your child for 5 seconds first. What are they looking at? What are they reaching for? Let their attention lead, then add language to it.

Activities
  • Two- to three-word sentences (EYLF 5): Encourage short phrases during play — 'I want ball,' 'big car go.' Model at snack: hold up the cup and wait for 'more juice' before pouring.
  • Label and describe (EYLF 5): Prompt naming with descriptors — 'red block,' 'big dog.' Parent narrates: 'You've got the big red one.'
  • Story songs (EYLF 5): Sing nursery rhymes with predictable endings — pause at the last word and wait for the child to fill it in.

VariationUse puppets to act out simple conversations. Children who won't speak to an adult will often speak to a puppet.

🪜 Scaffolding This Activity

Expansion technique: add one word to whatever the child says. Child: 'juice' → Parent: 'more juice.' Never skip two levels.

Expectant pause: stop, look expectantly, wait 5 seconds before modelling.

Accept all attempts: an approximation gets the same warm response as a clear production.

🎯 Facilitator Coaching Cues

When a parent says 'good talking!': "You could also reflect back what he said: 'You said big car! Here's the big car.' He gets the language and the reward."

When a parent models the full phrase first: "Let's try the pause first — hold up the cup, look at her expectantly, count to five."

When a child doesn't respond: "That's fine — he heard it. Input builds up before output appears."

👪 Parent Coaching Focus: Describe and Narrate

Parents learn to replace evaluative praise ('good talking!') with descriptive narration. The child gets both the language model and the natural consequence.

🏠 Try This at Home

At mealtimes, pause before pouring or passing anything. Wait for your child to communicate — even a point counts. When they do, add one word: 'more juice,' 'big apple.'

Equipment
Puppets for role playSimple storybooksSong/rhyme props (scarves, bells)

Cognitive Pod
Problem-Solving and Early Pretend Play
EF: IC (resist fixing it)
▾
Why This Matters

Shape sorters and puzzles build spatial reasoning, trial-and-error thinking, and persistence. Pretend play signals symbolic thinking — one object can represent another. Harvard Center on the Developing Child (2014): these activities exercise inhibitory control (resisting the urge to grab the answer) and working memory (holding the target shape in mind while rotating the piece).

🧠 Executive Function Skill This Week

Inhibitory Control (resist fixing it) + Working Memory (hold shape in mind)

Activities
  • Shape sorters and stacking rings (EYLF 4): Provide for trial-and-error. Resist guiding the child's hand. Celebrate persistence as much as success.
  • 2–3 piece inset puzzles (EYLF 4): Model turning a piece once slowly if stuck, then hand it back. The child needs to complete the placement themselves.
  • Pretend play with everyday props (EYLF 1): Cups, spoons, toy phones — follow the child's lead entirely. If they decide the cup is a hat, go with it.

VariationOffer choices during problem-solving ('Which piece goes here?') rather than pointing to the right spot.

🪜 Scaffolding This Activity

For shape sorters: if frustrated, reduce shapes to two or three, or help them feel the edges of the hole with a finger. Keep the problem-solving with the child.

For puzzles: model turning a piece once slowly, then hand it back.

For pretend play: follow completely. Symbolic play that the adult redirects is no longer child-led play.

🎯 Facilitator Coaching Cues

When a parent guides the child's hand: "Let's take our hands away — she's working something out. Even if she doesn't get it, that thinking is the learning."

When a parent redirects pretend play: "He's doing something sophisticated — he's decided the cup is something else. That's symbolic thinking. Let's see where he takes it."

When a child abandons a puzzle quickly: "Try leaving two pieces in and the third for her to find."

👪 Parent Coaching Focus: Follow Child's Lead

Parents learn that the child's struggle — even unsuccessful struggle — is the developmental work.

🏠 Try This at Home

Put a shape sorter somewhere accessible and let your child choose when to play. When they get stuck, try 'hmm, which one might fit?' and wait. The struggle is where the learning happens.

Equipment
Shape sorters and stacking rings2–3 piece inset puzzlesPretend play props (cups, spoons, toy phones)

Social-Emotional Pod
Parallel Play, Turn-Taking, and Emotion Naming
CASEL: Social Awareness + Relationship Skills
▾
Why This Matters

At 2 years, parallel play is developmentally appropriate and important — it is the precursor to cooperative play and should not be rushed. Naming emotions in books lays the groundwork for emotional literacy. Gottman (1997): simple emotional labelling is the most accessible Emotion Coaching step for parents new to the framework.

Activities
  • Parallel play — blocks, drawing (EYLF 2): Set up side-by-side activities and model sharing. Resist engineering interaction — proximity is enough.
  • Turn-taking — roll ball or car (EYLF 1): Simple back-and-forth with adult or peer. Name the turn: 'my turn... your turn.'
  • Emotion naming in books (EYLF 3): Point to faces in picture books and label them: 'he looks happy,' 'she's sad.' Don't require a verbal answer.

VariationUse mirrors for children to copy and label their own expressions.

🪜 Scaffolding This Activity

Parallel play: proximity first, interaction second. Children playing near each other are already succeeding.

Turn-taking: keep turns very short — 2-year-olds have a small window. Celebrate the wait as much as the action.

🎯 Facilitator Coaching Cues

When a parent engineers sharing: "At 2, playing near each other is the goal — you could narrate: 'You're both building!'"

When a child grabs a toy: "That's very normal — try narrating calmly: 'Jake wanted that one. Let's find another.'"

When a parent avoids emotion naming: "You can do this anywhere — next time she cries, try 'you look really sad about that' instead of fixing it immediately."

🌱 Development Note (Erikson)

Autonomy: the child who can identify their own feelings is beginning to understand themselves as a separate person with an inner life.

👪 Parent Coaching Focus: Warm Attention — Noticing Without Directing

Parents learn to be a warm, narrating presence — not a social director.

🏠 Try This at Home

At bedtime, point to faces in picture books and name the emotion. You don't need a response. Over weeks, you'll notice your child starting to point and name too.

Equipment
Mirrors for emotion playPicture books with facial expressionsCars or balls for turn-taking

Self-Help Pod
Fork Use, Handwashing, and Dressing Steps
ADL: Feeding + Hygiene + Dressing
▾
Why This Matters

Self-help skills at 2 years old are about independence, agency, and fine motor development in real-world contexts. Parents often do these things for children out of time pressure, inadvertently reducing practice opportunities.

Activities
  • Self-feeding — fork and open cup (EYLF 3): Encourage fork use for soft foods and open cup at mealtimes. The parent's role is to stabilise the bowl, not guide the utensil.
  • Handwashing before and after eating (EYLF 3): Practise as a genuine routine, not play. Use a consistent sequence: 'wet, soap, rub, rinse, dry.'
  • Dressing skills (EYLF 3): Support putting arms through sleeves and pushing feet into shoes. Hand-over-hand for the initial push, then release.

VariationRole play dressing and feeding dolls. Low-pressure practice — the child is teaching the doll, which gives a sense of competence.

🪜 Scaffolding This Activity

Fork use: (1) Loaded fork to child — they bring it to mouth. (2) Adult steadies bowl while child loads and transports. (3) Child fully independent.

Dressing: Start at whatever stage the child is at and move one step forward.

Handwashing: same words, same order, same location every time. Procedural memory develops through exact repetition.

🎯 Facilitator Coaching Cues

When a parent feeds every bite: "He can do more — let's try handing him the loaded fork and just steadying the bowl."

When a parent does the full dressing: "What if we got her arm most of the way through and let her pull it out?"

When a parent skips handwashing: "The routine is worth a lot. Even 30 seconds with soap counts."

⚠️ Common Parent Trap

The most common pattern is parents doing more than necessary because it is faster. The message: 5 extra minutes of supported independence now saves months of dependence later.

🌱 Development Note (Erikson)

Autonomy vs Shame and Doubt: every time a child completes the last step of a self-help task themselves, they build the experience of 'I can do this.' A parent who over-assists takes that experience away.

👪 Parent Coaching Focus: Support Then Release

Parents learn to identify the minimum level of support the child actually needs — and then reduce it.

🏠 Try This at Home

Choose one skill to focus on this week. Same words, same steps, same amount of help every time. Then give your child the last step to complete themselves.

Equipment
Toddler forks and open cupsSoap and handwashing stationDress-up dolls with clothing
