Week 10

Gross Motor Pod
Object Control: Catching
FMS: Catching
▾
Why This Matters

Catching is developmentally later than throwing and kicking. Balloons are the ideal starting tool because their slow descent gives more time to track and respond.

🏃 FMS Technique Cues (Munch & Move)
Hands out in front, fingers spread
Keep your eyes on the ball
Catch with both hands and bring ball to chest
Watch it all the way into your hands
Soft hands — don't bat it away
Activities
  • Balloon catch (EYLF 3): Adult tosses balloon from close range. Child watches it all the way.
  • Beach ball (EYLF 3): Larger and slower than standard ball.
  • Roll + catch (EYLF 3): Roll a ball along the ground — underarm catch.

VariationLarge scarf to 'catch' a balloon together — builds the catching schema with more success.

👪 Parent Coaching Focus: Scaffold and Step Back
🏠 Try This at Home

Balloon keep-up at home. Count how many hits before it lands.

🫀 Body Awareness This Week

When your child catches something or misses — watch their face. That surprise is proprioceptive feedback — their body expected something different. Narrate: "You got it! Did that feel different in your hands?"

Communication Pod
Naming Categories
🦉 OWL
▾
Why This Matters

Categorical language — understanding that 'dog', 'cat', and 'bird' are all 'animals' — accelerates vocabulary organisation.

🦉 OWL Reminder — Observe, Wait, Listen

OWL — Listen and expand: when your child names something, add the category: child: 'dog' — parent: 'yes, dog — it's an animal.'

Activities
  • Food sort game (EYLF 5): Real or plastic food sorted into 'eat' and 'don't eat.'
  • Picture sort (EYLF 4): Animals vs vehicles. Name the category before each item.
  • Farm animals group (EYLF 5): 'They're all animals — they all live on the farm.'

VariationPlay 'which one doesn't belong?' — builds category exclusion.

👪 Parent Coaching Focus: Describe and Narrate
🏠 Try This at Home

At the supermarket, name categories as you go: 'this is fruit, this is a vegetable, these are the cereals.'

📚 Book of the Week — Hanen Book Nook

Freight Train — Donald Crews

Vehicle and colour categories — matching the naming categories theme. Simple, bold illustrations make category sorting easy: "What else is red? What else has wheels?" Extend to real objects in the room.

Cognitive Pod
Sensory Exploration — Touch, Texture, Squish
EF: IC (sustain attention on a slow experience)
▾
Why This Matters

Harvard (2014): sensory activities build focused attention and the capacity to sustain engagement — precursors to inhibitory control.

🧠 Executive Function Skill This Week

Inhibitory Control (sustain attention on a slow experience) + Cognitive Flexibility (shift between sensory inputs)

Activities
  • Touch-feel books (EYLF 5): Books with different textures.
  • Sand play (EYLF 2): Free exploration first, then directed.
  • Sensory bag squish (EYLF 4): Zip-lock bag with gel — sealed. Squish and watch it move.

VariationFrozen water play — ice cubes in a tray.

👪 Parent Coaching Focus: Follow Child's Lead
🏠 Try This at Home

Make a sensory bag at home — zip-lock with hair gel, sealed with tape. Name what you notice: 'squishy, cold, smooth.'

Social-Emotional Pod
Empathy — Comforting Others
CASEL: Social Awareness
▾
Why This Matters

Empathy at 2 years is primarily expressed through imitation of caring behaviours. Genuine empathy toward peers begins to emerge around 24 months.

Activities
  • Feed doll (EYLF 1): 'Baby is hungry. How can we help?'
  • Comfort toy 'hurt' (EYLF 1): 'Teddy fell and got hurt. What should we do?'
  • Clap peer sharing (EYLF 2): When a child shares, the group claps and names it.

VariationBooks where characters have big feelings — talk about how to help.

🌱 Development Note (Erikson)

The child who can recognise and respond to another's distress is making one of the most significant social cognitive leaps of early childhood.

👪 Parent Coaching Focus: Emotion Coach — Other-Focused

Parents begin to notice and name emotions in others, modelling social awareness.

🏠 Try This at Home

When your child sees someone upset this week, ask: 'how do you think they feel? What could we do?' Wait for any response.

Equipment
Doll with propsBooks about big feelings

Self-Help Pod
Toileting Awareness
ADL: Toileting
▾
Why This Matters

This week addresses cognitive readiness only — building familiarity without any expectation of performance.

Activities
  • Sit on potty play (EYLF 3): Clothed sitting on potty during play — completely optional and pressure-free.
  • Doll toileting (EYLF 3): Doll uses the potty in play.
  • Picture book about toilet (EYLF 5): Normalise the concept through story.

VariationMake a 'toilet sequence' picture card (4 pictures: sit → wipe → flush → wash).

🎯 Facilitator Coaching Cues

When a parent mentions toilet training: "Readiness is three things — physical control, understanding, and willingness. We can build understanding this week. Control and willingness need to come from your child."

When a child refuses to sit: "That's completely fine — the goal is just familiarity with the object."

⚠️ Common Parent Trap

Children who are developmentally ready typically train in days; children who are rushed often take months. Positive association and familiarity are the most useful contributions parents can make now.

🌱 Development Note (Erikson)

Autonomy is highly sensitive in toileting — children who experience pressure or shame around toileting show delayed readiness. This week is entirely about familiarity and positive association.

👪 Parent Coaching Focus: Follow Child's Lead — No Pressure

Parents understand that toileting readiness cannot be forced.

🏠 Try This at Home

Put a potty in the bathroom and let your child explore it as an object this week — no pressure to use it.
