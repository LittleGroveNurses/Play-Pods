Gross Motor Pod
Object Control: Overarm Throwing
FMS: Overarm Throw
▾
Why This Matters

Overarm throwing requires trunk rotation and weight transfer. The key cue this week: 'step and throw' — stepping forward on the opposite foot. This is the foundation of all later throwing.

🏃 FMS Technique Cues (Munch & Move)
Point to your target
Swing arm back, step and throw
Step forward on opposite foot to throwing hand
Follow through across body towards hip
Eyes on the target
Activities
  • Beanbags in basket (EYLF 3): Large basket, close range. Any release counts.
  • Soft balls at target (EYLF 4): Soft target on wall. Step toward it and throw.
  • Throw/catch game (EYLF 3): Adult catches, narrates, returns.

VariationScrunch newspaper into 'snowballs' — lightweight and easy to handle.

👪 Parent Coaching Focus: Scaffold and Step Back
🏠 Try This at Home

Throw soft balls at a target taped to the wall. Count how many land on it.

Equipment
BeanbagsSoft ballsTarget (basket or paper on wall)

Communication Pod
Following Two-Step Directions
🦉 OWL
▾
Why This Matters

Following two-step directions is a direct working memory task — the child must hold both parts of the instruction in mind while completing the first.

🦉 OWL Reminder — Observe, Wait, Listen

OWL — Observe before you instruct. Watch what your child is already doing before giving a direction.

Activities
  • 'Pick up block, give to me' (EYLF 4): Simple two-step with familiar objects.
  • Cleanup 2 steps (EYLF 3): 'Pick up the car, put it in the box.' Natural routine context.
  • Action song 2 steps (EYLF 3): 'Clap your hands, then stomp your feet.'

VariationUse picture cue cards to show both steps visually.

👪 Parent Coaching Focus: Describe and Narrate
🏠 Try This at Home

Give one two-step direction at cleanup time each day. Wait to see if both steps happen before helping.

Equipment
Familiar toysPicture cue cards (optional)

Matching — Pairs and Picture Cards
EF: WM (hold target in mind)
▾
Why This Matters

Harvard (2014): matching games can be extended by changing the rule — match by colour, then by shape — to directly build cognitive flexibility.

🧠 Executive Function Skill This Week

Working Memory (hold target in mind) + Cognitive Flexibility (match by colour, then by shape)

Activities
  • Pair blocks (EYLF 4): Match by colour first, then by size.
  • Match picture cards (EYLF 4): Simple identical-pairs card game.
  • Collect 2 same toys (EYLF 5): 'Find me two red things.'

VariationChange the matching rule mid-activity.

👪 Parent Coaching Focus: Follow Child's Lead
🏠 Try This at Home

Matching game with household objects — two spoons, two red cups, two socks. Let your child find the pairs.

Equipment
Matching blocksIdentical picture card pairs

Social-Emotional Pod
Expressing Emotions — Mirror, Label, Song
CASEL: Self-Awareness
▾
Why This Matters

CASEL self-awareness at this age is specifically about recognising and naming one's own emotions. Gottman (1997): labelling emotions builds the neural vocabulary for self-regulation. Children who are consistently given emotion words show stronger self-management by age 5.

Activities
  • Mirror happy/sad (EYLF 3): Make faces in mirror together. 'That's your happy face!'
  • Label feelings (EYLF 5): In the moment, name the child's emotion.
  • Emotion song (EYLF 5): 'If you're happy and you know it' with added verses.

VariationUse an 'emotion wheel' poster with simple faces.

🎯 Facilitator Coaching Cues

When a parent says 'don't cry, it's okay': "Try 'you feel really sad' instead — naming it actually calms the nervous system faster than reassurance."

🌱 Development Note (Erikson)

Self-awareness: recognising 'I am happy' or 'I am angry' is the first step in the autonomy task.

👪 Parent Coaching Focus: Emotion Coach

Parents begin using Gottman's five steps, starting with step 1 (notice) and step 4 (label).

🏠 Try This at Home

Use the emotion song at home. When your child is having a big feeling, try naming it out loud before you try to fix it.

Equipment
MirrorsEmotion wheel poster

Dressing — Arms Through Sleeves and Shoes
ADL: Dressing
▾
Why This Matters

Dressing independence develops in a predictable sequence: undressing before dressing, larger items before small. Arms through sleeves are appropriate 2-year-old targets.

Activities
  • Push arms through sleeves (EYLF 3): Adult starts sleeve, child pushes arm through.
  • Pull up socks (EYLF 3): Place sock over toes — child pulls up.
  • Dress-up dolls (EYLF 3): Dressing a doll removes performance pressure.

VariationElastic-waist pants are easiest to start.

⚠️ Common Parent Trap

Parents complete the action because they're running late. Frame it as: the 2 minutes invested in letting them try saves 5 years of dressing for them.

🌱 Development Note (Erikson)

Autonomy: dressing is one of the most powerful arenas for a 2-year-old's sense of self. Every step completed independently contributes to 'I am a person who can manage my own body.'

👪 Parent Coaching Focus: Support Then Release
🏠 Try This at Home

Every morning this week, let your child push their own arms through sleeves. 'You do the pushing, I'll hold it open.'

Equipment
Dress-up clothes with easy fasteningsDoll with clothing
