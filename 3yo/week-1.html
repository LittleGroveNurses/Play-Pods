Week 1

Week 1 introduces 3-year-olds to early sentence use, structured gross motor play through jumping, and problem-solving through object permanence and memory games. Activities are scaffolded for older toddlers' increased stamina, social awareness, and independence. The focus is on communication in context, balance and coordination, and social-emotional development through peer and caregiver interaction.

Gross Motor Pod
Locomotion: Jumping (Two-Footed)
FMS: Two-Footed Jump
▾
Why This Matters

At 3 years, two-footed jumping is the foundational locomotion target — it requires bilateral coordination, timing, and the arm-swing-bend-land sequence. Drumbeat jumps simultaneously build inhibitory control (the EF skill of stopping): the child must hold the impulse to jump until the beat stops. Munch & Move (2018): 'swing your arms back and bend your knees, reach for the stars, bend knees to land.'

🏃 FMS Technique Cues (Munch & Move)
Swing arms back and bend your knees before jumping
Jump with both feet together
Reach for the stars on take-off
Bend knees to land — soft landing
Look where you're landing
Activities
  • Hopscotch squares (EYLF 3): Taped or chalk squares on the floor. Jump forward landing with both feet. Start close together, gradually widen the gap.
  • Kangaroo jumps (EYLF 3): Crouch with hands on chest, spring forward. Count consecutive jumps — 3 is a great target at this age.
  • Drumbeat jumps (EYLF 5): Beat a drum continuously — children move freely. When the beat stops, they freeze. Builds IC alongside motor skill.
  • Postural control (EYLF 3): Sitting upright on cushions during circle time, holding body steady during play. Proprioceptive foundation for later balance.
  • Fine motor — crayons + pegboards (EYLF 4): Grip development and hand-eye coordination as complement to gross motor session.

VariationFreeze in different poses when the drum stops — 'freeze like a statue, a tree, a robot.'

🪜 Scaffolding This Activity

Jumping: (1) Both feet hop in place. (2) Jump forward off a low step onto a mat. (3) Jump from square to square on hopscotch. (4) Jump with increasing distance between squares.

Watch for the arm swing — many 3-year-olds jump stiff-armed initially. Model the swing deliberately: 'arms back... and JUMP!'

🎯 Facilitator Coaching Cues

When a parent catches the child before landing: "Let's give her room to land herself — the wobble is part of learning the balance."

When a child lands on one foot: "He's getting there — knees together before you jump, then land together. Let's try it slowly."

When a child won't jump: "Try jumping together — parallel modelling works really well at 3. Side by side, no expectation on him."

👪 Parent Coaching Focus: Scaffold and Step Back

Parents learn that the wobble, the off-balance landing, and the attempt that doesn't quite work are all learning. Their role is to be nearby, narrate, and celebrate any attempt.

🏠 Try This at Home

Tape squares on the floor at home — or use cushion 'lily pads.' Jump from pad to pad. Count together. 'Arms back, then jump!' Try the drumbeat game with clapping instead.

🫀 Body Awareness This Week

After jumping, ask: "What does your body feel like now? Bouncy? Heavy? Warm?" — beginning to build a vocabulary for physical states. At 3, children can start connecting words to felt sensations when we give them the language.

Communication Pod
Two-Word Phrases in Context
🦉 OWL
▾
Why This Matters

Many 3-year-olds entering Play Pods have communication delays — starting at two-word combinations meets children where they are rather than where typical milestones say they should be. The principle is always: model one level above what the child is currently producing. If they use single words, we model two words. If they use two words, we model three.

🦉 OWL Reminder — Observe, Wait, Listen

OWL — Observe, Wait, Listen: This week, before you prompt or model anything, watch your child for 5 seconds. What are they pointing at? What are they reaching for? Let their attention lead — then add language to it.

Activities
  • Snack time two-word requests (EYLF 5): Hold up items and wait — model 'more juice' when the child points. Pause before pouring.
  • Doll play (EYLF 5): Use dolls in role play to encourage describing actions: 'baby sleep,' 'baby eat,' 'doll fall.'
  • Picture book prompts (EYLF 5): Read familiar books, pause at predictable moments, and wait for the child to fill in the phrase.
  • Puppets (EYLF 5): Puppets ask children two-word questions and wait for a two-word response. Children who won't speak to adults will often speak to puppets.

VariationUse puppets to reinforce two-word combinations. The puppet says 'big ball?' and waits — child responds.

🪜 Scaffolding This Activity

Expansion: always add one word to whatever the child says. 'Ball' → 'big ball.' 'Juice' → 'more juice.' Never jump two levels.

Expectant pause: hold up the item, make eye contact, wait 5 seconds. This is more powerful than modelling first.

Self-talk: narrate your own actions in two-word phrases: 'get cup,' 'pour juice,' 'sit down.'

🎯 Facilitator Coaching Cues

When a parent accepts pointing without prompting language: "Try holding the cup just out of reach and looking at her expectantly — give her 5 seconds before pouring."

When a parent corrects pronunciation: "At this stage, getting the words is the goal — the sounds will sharpen as her vocabulary grows. Every approximation gets the same warm response."

When a child uses single words: "He's using words — that's great. Try adding one more: 'yes, red ball.' You're modelling the next step without correcting what he said."

👪 Parent Coaching Focus: Describe and Narrate

Parents replace evaluative responses ('good talking!') with descriptive narration ('you said big ball!'). The child gets the language model and the natural consequence.

🏠 Try This at Home

At every meal this week, pause before pouring or passing anything. Wait for your child to communicate — even a point counts. When they do, add one word: 'more juice, yes!' Keep it playful.

Cognitive Pod
Object Permanence — Memory and Hide-and-Find
EF: WM (track where object went)
▾
Why This Matters

Object permanence — understanding that objects continue to exist when not seen — is the cognitive foundation for all memory tasks. For 3-year-olds, the challenge is tracking an object across multiple moves (the shell game). Harvard Center (2014): hiding games exercise working memory as the child remembers what was there and mentally tracks the move. Inhibitory control is also exercised: the child must resist checking the wrong cup.

🧠 Executive Function Skill This Week

Working Memory (track where object went) + Inhibitory Control (resist checking too soon)

Activities
  • Hide toy under one cup (EYLF 4): Three cups face-down; hide a small toy under one. Shuffle slowly. Can the child track it?
  • Peek-a-boo with scarf (EYLF 5): Cover objects or faces with scarves, prompting children to anticipate the reveal. At 3, add a delay before uncovering.
  • Hide-and-seek toy in visible location (EYLF 1): Place a toy in a partially hidden but visible location. Encourage searching and naming location words: 'behind the box,' 'under the table.'

VariationAdd complexity by hiding two toys at once under different cups — can the child remember where each one is?

🪜 Scaffolding This Activity

Start with one cup, one toy, no shuffle. Then shuffle once slowly. Then twice. Build the complexity step by step.

For children who check too soon: try a '1, 2, 3, now look!' structure — the counting gives them an inhibitory hold.

🎯 Facilitator Coaching Cues

When a parent points to the correct cup: "Let's wait and let her find it — even if she checks the wrong one first, that's information she can use."

When a child lifts all cups at once: "He's working out the game — try putting your hand lightly on his to slow him down. 'Which one? Just one.'"

👪 Parent Coaching Focus: Follow Child's Lead

Parents learn that their role in cognitive play is to make the problem slightly harder — not to solve it, and not to praise the answer rather than the thinking.

🏠 Try This at Home

Play the cup game at home — three cups, a small toy, shuffle slowly. Start with one shuffle. Let your child watch carefully. Make it a game: 'keep your eyes on the toy...'

Equipment
Three cups (opaque)Small toys for hidingScarves for peek-a-boo

Social-Emotional Pod
Attachment and Secure Base in Group Setting
CASEL: Self-Management
▾
Why This Matters

Week 1 of any group program is fundamentally about safety and belonging. For 3-year-olds with developmental vulnerability, anxiety about new settings and unfamiliar people is common and healthy. The social-emotional goal is to establish the group as a secure base — not to push social interaction before the child feels safe enough to engage.

Gottman (1997): step 1 is simply noticing and acknowledging the emotion. Step 3 is sitting with it — not fixing it. This week is entirely steps 1 and 3.

Activities
  • Cuddle toy during transitions (EYLF 1): Allow children to bring or choose a soft toy for transition times. Name it and give it a role in the group.
  • Peek-a-boo with caregiver (EYLF 1): Simple adult-initiated peek-a-boo builds trust and playful connection before more complex peer interaction.
  • Hand-holding in pairs (EYLF 2): Pair children intentionally for circle time or walking transitions. Keep pairs consistent week to week.

VariationPair children intentionally to foster early friendship and cooperation — consistent pairings across weeks 1–4.

🪜 Scaffolding This Activity

Week 1: the facilitator's main job is warmth and low demand. No child should feel required to perform or participate beyond their comfort level.

Parents who are anxious transmit that anxiety — coach parents to project calm confidence even if they feel uncertain.

🎯 Facilitator Coaching Cues

When a child clings: "This is healthy attachment — she's using you as a safe base to observe from. The exploring will come. Try narrating what you both see: 'there's another child jumping — shall we watch?'"

When a parent pushes peer interaction: "At 3, being in the same space as peers is already a social achievement. Let's not rush the interaction — just narrate what's happening around him."

🌱 Development Note (Erikson)

Initiative vs Guilt: 3-year-olds are beginning to test the boundaries of their social world. A week 1 that feels safe — where initiative is welcomed and mistakes are met with warmth — sets the tone for the entire program.

👪 Parent Coaching Focus: Safe Base — Project Calm

Parents understand that their own regulated presence is the most powerful social-emotional scaffold they can offer in week 1. Their job is to be a calm, warm anchor.

🏠 Try This at Home

Talk about the group at home: 'we went to Play Pods today. There were other children jumping.' Keep it simple and positive. Ask: 'what did you see?' Not 'did you make a friend?'

Soft cuddle toysSpace for circle time and paired walking

Self-Help Pod
Spoon Feeding Independence
ADL: Feeding
▾
Why This Matters

By 3 years, most children can use a spoon for semi-solid foods with reasonable accuracy. For children with developmental vulnerability, spoon use may still be emerging. The goal this week is to establish the baseline — where is this child on the spoon-use continuum — and set the right scaffold level for the program.

Activities
  • Spoon feeding (EYLF 3): Provide opportunities to eat with a spoon independently. Observe and scaffold from the child's current level.
  • Hand-over-hand support (EYLF 3): For children who need it — guide the spoon hand but release before the bowl reaches the mouth. The child must do the final delivery.
  • Pretend feeding doll (EYLF 1): Children feed dolls using toy utensils, practising daily routines through play with lower stakes.

VariationEncourage children to 'help' feed peers' dolls — builds social play into self-help practice.

🪜 Scaffolding This Activity

Observe first: (1) Child brings loaded spoon to mouth — steady the bowl only. (2) Child can load from bowl — no support needed. (3) Child starting from scratch — hand-over-hand for loading, release for transport.

Never complete the final delivery for the child — always release before mouth.

🎯 Facilitator Coaching Cues

When a parent spoon-feeds a capable 3-year-old: "Let's see what she can do if we give her the spoon and step back. Even if she gets it messy — that's how the skill builds."

When a child refuses the spoon: "Try the doll feeding first — sometimes practice in a no-pressure context transfers to the real thing within a few minutes."

⚠️ Common Parent Trap

At 3, parents often continue spoon-feeding because it's faster and less messy. The framing: 'every meal where they use the spoon themselves is a meal that builds towards independence at school entry.'

🌱 Development Note (Erikson)

Autonomy continues alongside new Initiative: the 3-year-old who can feed themselves is not just practising a skill — they are developing a narrative about who they are: 'I am someone who can do things.'

👪 Parent Coaching Focus: Support Then Release

Parents identify the minimum level of support this child actually needs for spoon use — and commit to that level consistently across all meals.

🏠 Try This at Home

One meal a day this week where your child uses the spoon themselves — even for part of it. Steady the bowl if needed. Step back for everything else. Bibs and a floor mat make the mess manageable.
