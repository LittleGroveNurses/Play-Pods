Gross Motor Pod
Object Control: Overarm Throwing
FMS: Overarm Throw
▾
Why This Matters

At 3 years, overarm throwing is developing from a pushing action to a more mature rotation pattern. The critical cues: opposite-foot step (cross-lateral coordination) and follow-through. These two elements are not yet automatic — they need to be explicitly modelled and practised. Diamond (2000): the cross-lateral coordination in throwing is strongly linked to the same neural circuits as reading and attention.

🏃 FMS Technique Cues (Munch & Move)
Point to your target with the non-throwing hand
Swing arm back — elbow up
Step forward on opposite foot to throwing hand
Follow through across body towards opposite hip
Eyes on the target all the way through
Activities
  • Beanbags into basket (EYLF 3): Large basket, close range. 'Point, step, throw.' Any release that leaves the hand counts.
  • Soft balls at wall target (EYLF 4): Coloured circles on a wall or fence. 'Point to the circle, then throw at it.' Introduce the pointing cue explicitly.
  • Partner throw/catch (EYLF 3): Partner standing close. Adult catches every throw — the 3-year-old is practising the throw, not the catch.

VariationBombardment: two teams, cones as a dividing line, throw bean bags to the other side. Pure throwing volume — great for repetition.

👪 Parent Coaching Focus: Scaffold and Step Back
🏠 Try This at Home

Throw soft balls at a target taped to a wall. 'Point, step, throw!' Try 'can you hit the red circle?' to add some aim. Take turns — model the technique yourself.

BeanbagsSoft ballsTarget (basket or wall circles)Cones for bombardment

Communication Pod
Following Two- and Three-Step Directions
🦉 OWL
▾
Why This Matters

Two-step directions are a direct working memory task. At 3 years, children can begin to hold three-step sequences in working memory — particularly when the sequence is embedded in a familiar routine (pack up → wash hands → sit at table). This is precisely why routines matter: the repetition means the sequence transfers from working memory to procedural memory.

🦉 OWL Reminder — Observe, Wait, Listen

OWL — Observe before you instruct. Watch what your child is already doing before giving a direction — sometimes the direction can follow their action rather than interrupt it.

Activities
  • 'Pick up block, put in box' (EYLF 4): Simple two-step with familiar objects. Then extend: 'pick up the block, put it in the box, then come sit down.'
  • Two-step action song (EYLF 3): 'Clap your hands, then stomp your feet.' Extend to three steps.
  • Three-step cleanup routine (EYLF 3): Pack up toys → wash hands → come to table. The familiar routine makes the three steps manageable.

VariationPicture cue cards showing each step — visual support for children who need it alongside the verbal direction.

👪 Parent Coaching Focus: Describe and Narrate
🏠 Try This at Home

Give one three-step direction at cleanup time: 'pick up your shoes, put them by the door, then come to the table.' Wait to see all three steps before helping.

Equipment
Familiar toysPicture cue cards for steps (optional)Cleanup basket

Cognitive Pod
Matching and Sorting with Rule-Switching
EF: WM (hold target in mind)
▾
Why This Matters

Matching is the simplest categorical thinking task. The key 3-year-old extension is rule-switching: first sort by colour, then immediately switch to sort by size or shape. Harvard (2014): children who successfully switch sorting rules between the ages of 3 and 3.5 are demonstrating cognitive flexibility — the EF skill most strongly linked to school readiness and social problem-solving. Blakey et al. (2015): this specific flexibility develops rapidly between 3 and 3.5 years.

🧠 Executive Function Skill This Week

Working Memory (hold target in mind) + Cognitive Flexibility (switch from colour rule to shape rule)

Activities
  • Pair blocks by colour (EYLF 4): Sort into colour groups. Fast success — builds confidence before the harder task.
  • Match picture cards (EYLF 4): Simple identical pairs. Can extend to 'find ones that are the same colour' vs 'find ones that are the same shape.'
  • Sort by colour, then switch to size (EYLF 5): After completing the colour sort — 'now let's sort them differently — by big and small.' This is the CF challenge.

Variation'Which one doesn't belong?' — three animals and a car. Name why. Builds categorical exclusion and early reasoning.

👪 Parent Coaching Focus: Follow Child's Lead
🏠 Try This at Home

Sort the laundry or fruit bowl together — first by colour, then switch: 'now let's sort by big and small.' The rule switch is the challenge — notice if they can do it!

Equipment
Coloured blocksIdentical picture card pairsMixed household objects for sorting

Social-Emotional Pod
Expressing Emotions — Label, Mirror, Song
CASEL: Self-Awareness
▾
Why This Matters

CASEL self-awareness at 3 years is specifically about identifying and naming emotions in oneself. Gottman (1997): consistent emotion labelling over weeks builds the neural vocabulary for self-regulation. At 3, children can name and recognise at least 4–6 emotions — adding surprised, scared, and frustrated to happy, sad, and angry. The mirror is a powerful tool because it gives immediate visual feedback.

Activities
  • Mirror faces (EYLF 3): Make faces together in mirror — happy, sad, angry, surprised, scared. Name each one.
  • Label feelings in the moment (EYLF 5): When a child is experiencing an emotion during the session: 'you look really frustrated — this bit is hard.'
  • 'If You're Happy and You Know It' extended (EYLF 5): Add verses for sad, angry, surprised, scared. Use body actions for each emotion.

VariationEmotion wheel poster with 6 faces — child points to how they feel at arrival, mid-session, and end-of-session.

🎯 Facilitator Coaching Cues

When a parent says 'don't cry, it's okay': "Try 'you look really sad' instead — labelling it actually activates the language cortex and calms the emotional response faster than reassurance."

When a parent dismisses an emotion: "Even if the emotion seems disproportionate to us — it's real to her. The label validates it: 'you feel really angry about that.'"

🌱 Development Note (Erikson)

Initiative: the child who can name their own emotions is beginning to develop self-reflection — the capacity to observe their own inner states. This is the psychological foundation of agency.

👪 Parent Coaching Focus: Emotion Coach

Parents begin systematically applying Gottman steps 1 (notice) and 4 (label) across the session and at home. These two steps are the foundation everything else builds on.

🏠 Try This at Home

Use the emotion song at home this week — add your own verses. When your child is having a big feeling, try naming it out loud before you try to fix it. 'You feel really angry.'

MirrorsEmotion wheel poster (6 faces)Emotion song

Self-Help Pod
Dressing — Arms Through Sleeves, Hats
ADL: Dressing
▾
Why This Matters

Dressing independence develops in a predictable sequence: undressing before dressing, larger items before smaller. At 3, arms through sleeves and hats are the appropriate targets. The dress-up context removes performance pressure and allows high-repetition practice in a play frame.

Activities
  • Push arm through sleeve (EYLF 3): Adult starts the sleeve opening, child pushes arm through and pulls out. The final step belongs to the child.
  • Hats on/off (EYLF 3): Rule-based: 'hat outside, no hat inside.' Consistent prompt every time entering and exiting.
  • Dress-up role play (EYLF 1): Costumes and dress-up clothes in the play area. High repetition of putting on and taking off in a playful, low-stakes context.

VariationIntroduce the concept of weather-appropriate dressing: 'it's cold today — what do we need to wear?'

⚠️ Common Parent Trap

Parents complete dressing to save time, especially in the morning. Reframe: 'the 3 minutes invested in letting them try now builds 10 years of dressing independence. It's worth it.'

🌱 Development Note (Erikson)

Initiative: at 3, dressing is about more than the skill — it's about the child beginning to make decisions about what they want to wear. This sense of initiative over appearance is developmentally important and should be welcomed, not overridden.

👪 Parent Coaching Focus: Support Then Release
🏠 Try This at Home

Every morning, let your child push their own arms through sleeves — hold the opening, they do the pushing. And at every indoor-outdoor transition: wait for them to manage their own hat.

Equipment
Dress-up clothes with easy fasteningsHats in different stylesFull-length mirror
