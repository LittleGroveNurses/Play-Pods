Week 10

Week 10 focuses on naming categories in communication, catching as the object control FMS, sensory exploration in cognitive, empathy and comforting in social-emotional, and toileting awareness in self-help. At 3, catching becomes a realistic target with the right equipment (balloons, beach balls).

Gross Motor Pod
Object Control: Catching
FMS: Catching
▾
Why This Matters

Catching is developmentally later than throwing and kicking — full catching competence doesn't emerge until 5–6 years. At 3, the realistic target is a two-handed trap of a large, slow-moving object. Balloons are the ideal starting tool because their slow descent provides maximum tracking time. Munch & Move (2018): 'hands out in front, eyes on the ball, catch with both hands and bring to chest.'

🏃 FMS Technique Cues (Munch & Move)
Hands out in front — fingers spread and soft
Keep your eyes on the ball all the way
Catch with both hands and bring to chest
Watch it into your hands — not past your hands
Soft hands — cushion it, don't slap it away
Activities
  • Balloon catch (EYLF 3): Adult tosses from very close range. Child watches it all the way into their hands.
  • Beach ball partner catch (EYLF 3): Larger and slower than a standard ball — more success, more motivation.
  • Roll and stop (EYLF 3): Roll a ball along the ground. Child stops it with both hands. Easier than aerial — good confidence builder.

VariationLarge scarf or towel catch — hold corners together, use it like a net to catch a balloon. Reduces coordination demand while maintaining the tracking skill.

👪 Parent Coaching Focus: Scaffold and Step Back
🏠 Try This at Home

Balloon keep-up at home — keep the balloon off the floor as long as possible. Count how many hits before it lands. Even 3 in a row is impressive.

🫀 Body Awareness This Week

Ask children: "When you miss the ball, does your body feel different to when you catch it? What does that feel like?" This is interoceptive feedback from motor success and failure — teaching children that their body gives them information about what just happened.

Equipment
BalloonsBeach ballLarge scarf or towel (variation)

Communication Pod
Naming Categories — Grouping and Explaining
🦉 OWL
▾
Why This Matters

Categorical language accelerates vocabulary organisation and is directly linked to reading comprehension. At 3, children can begin to understand and use category labels ('animal,' 'food,' 'vehicle') and explain group membership: 'they're all animals because they're alive.' The reasoning — 'because they all...' — is the critical language extension this week.

🦉 OWL Reminder — Observe, Wait, Listen

OWL — Listen and expand: when your child names something, add the category: 'yes, dog — it's an animal.' Then ask: 'what else is an animal?'

Activities
  • Food sort — fruit vs other (EYLF 5): Sort real or plastic food. Name the category: 'these are all fruit.'
  • Animal/vehicle picture sort (EYLF 4): Name the category before each placement. Then explain: 'why does this one go here?'
  • Farm animal grouping (EYLF 5): Collect all farm animals. 'They're all animals and they all live on a farm.'

Variation'Which one doesn't belong?' — three farm animals and a car. Name why. Builds categorical exclusion and early reasoning.

👪 Parent Coaching Focus: Describe and Narrate
🏠 Try This at Home

At the supermarket or kitchen: 'these are all vegetables,' 'these are all cereals.' Ask: 'what else is a vegetable?' Building the internal category structure for reading and maths.

Sensory Exploration — Texture, Temperature, Proprioception
EF: IC (sustain focus on a slow experience)
▾
Why This Matters

Sensory exploration at 3 builds proprioceptive and tactile discrimination — the body knowledge underpinning fine motor control, spatial awareness, and self-regulation. Harvard (2014): sensory activities build focused attention and the capacity to sustain engagement — precursors to inhibitory control. At 3, the language extension is explicitly naming sensory properties: 'squishy, rough, smooth, cold, heavy, light.'

🧠 Executive Function Skill This Week

Inhibitory Control (sustain focus on a slow experience) + Cognitive Flexibility (shift between sensory channels)

Activities
  • Touch-feel books (EYLF 5): Books with different textures. Stroke, rub, scratch. Name each texture: 'rough, smooth, bumpy, soft.'
  • Sand and water play (EYLF 2): Free exploration first — follow what the child does. Then directed: 'fill the cup, pour it out, pat it flat.' Name what happens.
  • Sensory bag squish (EYLF 4): Sealed zip-lock bag with gel or hair gel. Squish and watch. 'What does it feel like? What happens when you press hard?'

VariationIce cube tray — cold and fascinating. Name the temperature: 'cold, icy, wet, slippery.' And watch it melt.

👪 Parent Coaching Focus: Follow Child's Lead
🏠 Try This at Home

Make a sensory bag at home — zip-lock with hair gel, sealed with tape. Name what you notice together: 'squishy, cold, smooth.' What happens when you press it hard?

Equipment
Touch-feel booksSand traySensory bagsIce (optional)

Social-Emotional Pod
Empathy — Recognising and Responding to Others
CASEL: Social Awareness
▾
Why This Matters

Empathy at 3 years extends from passive recognition ('he's sad') to responsive action ('he's sad — I should help him'). This is CASEL social awareness at its most developmentally significant for this age group. The key facilitation skill is noticing and narrating moments of empathy when they occur spontaneously — the most powerful reinforcer is the immediate social narration.

Activities
  • Feed 'hungry' doll (EYLF 1): 'Baby is hungry. How can we help?' Child feeds the doll.
  • Comfort 'hurt' toy (EYLF 1): 'Teddy fell and got hurt — he's crying. What should we do?' Model comforting behaviour.
  • Celebrate peer sharing (EYLF 2): When a child shares spontaneously, stop the group and name it: '[name] shared their blocks with [name]! That was kind.'

VariationBooks where characters have big feelings — read together and ask 'how do you think they feel? What would you do if you were there?'

🌱 Development Note (Erikson)

Social initiative: the 3-year-old who responds to another's distress without being prompted is demonstrating one of the most significant social-moral achievements of this developmental stage.

👪 Parent Coaching Focus: Emotion Coach — Other-Focused

Parents begin to notice and name emotions in others — including other children in the group — modelling social awareness as an outward-looking practice, not just inward.

🏠 Try This at Home

When your child sees someone upset this week — a sibling, a friend, a character in a book — ask: 'how do you think they feel? What could we do?' Wait for any response. Celebrate it.

Equipment
Dolls with propsBooks with big feelingsSoft toys for comfort role-play

Self-Help Pod
Toileting Awareness
ADL: Toileting
▾
Why This Matters

At 3, toileting readiness varies enormously. Some children will already be trained; others are not yet ready. This week addresses children who are not yet trained, focusing entirely on cognitive readiness (understanding what the toilet is for) and positive association. Any performance pressure accelerates resistance.

Activities
  • Sit on potty clothed (EYLF 3): Optional, completely pressure-free. Introduce the object in a play frame.
  • Doll toileting play (EYLF 1): Doll uses the potty in play. Names the sequence: 'sit, wait, wipe, flush, wash.' No expectation on the child.
  • 4-picture sequence card (EYLF 5): Sit → wipe → flush → wash. Build procedural knowledge without any performance expectation.

VariationA child's book about learning to use the toilet — normalise through story, not instruction.

🎯 Facilitator Coaching Cues

When a parent reports they're toilet training: "The three readiness signs to watch for: staying dry for 2+ hours, knowing when they've gone, and showing interest. When all three are present, it usually takes 2–3 days. Without all three, it can take months of stress."

When a child refuses the potty: "That's completely fine — no pressure. The goal is familiarity and positive association only."

⚠️ Common Parent Trap

Parents begin training before readiness signs are present. Reframe: waiting for genuine readiness and training in 2–3 days is less stressful for everyone than premature training that takes months.

🌱 Development Note (Erikson)

Autonomy remains sensitive in toileting at 3 — children who experience pressure or shame show delayed readiness. This week is about cognitive readiness and positive association only. Initiative in toileting — when it comes — must be entirely child-initiated.

👪 Parent Coaching Focus: Follow Child's Lead — No Pressure

Parents understand that toileting readiness cannot be forced, and that positive association is the only useful contribution they can make before the child is ready.

🏠 Try This at Home

Put a potty in the bathroom and let your child explore it as an object — sit on it clothed, put a toy on it, talk about it. No pressure. Just positive familiarity.
