Week 9

Gross Motor Pod
Fine Motor: Utensil Use — Fork, Scoop, Tong Transfer, Playdough
FMS: Precision Tool Use
▾
Why This Matters

At 3, utensil use targets the precision grip and wrist supination needed for full tool independence by school entry. This week adds tong transfer as a progressive challenge beyond spoon scooping — tongs require a fundamentally different grip pattern (lateral pinch/squeeze rather than cupping) and a more demanding wrist-finger coordination. The Montessori Practical Life curriculum identifies tong transfer as appropriate from self-feeding age and particularly valuable for 3-year-olds who have mastered spoon use. Playdough provides the ideal practice medium — no consequence for dropping. The pretend kitchen context builds motivation through role-play while practising the same fine motor skills.

🏃 FMS Technique Cues (Munch & Move)
Hold fork with three fingers forming a tripod
Point tines down toward food
Elbow slightly elevated on transport to mouth
Eyes on the food — hand follows eyes
Tong transfer: squeeze with fingers and thumb — soft grip, not a squeeze
Activities
  • Fork with soft fruit (EYLF 3): Soft fruit pieces on a plate. Fork independently — adult steadies the plate only if needed.
  • Tong transfer — pom poms or cotton balls (EYLF 4): Two bowls, a set of child-sized tongs, and 10–15 pom poms or cotton balls. Transfer from one bowl to the other. Count as you go. The squeeze-and-lift action is entirely different from spooning — stronger IC required (resist squeezing too hard) and more precision finger control.
  • Playdough scooping (EYLF 4): Small spoon to transfer playdough between two containers. Count the scoops. A familiar activity from earlier weeks — now done with increased independence and speed.
  • Pretend kitchen utensils (EYLF 4): Real wooden spoon, spatula, tongs — pretend cooking builds grip strength and tool knowledge across multiple utensil types.

VariationTraining chopsticks for any 3-year-old who has mastered tongs — they love the challenge and the self-determination of trying something 'hard.' Begin with larger objects (pom poms) before smaller ones.

👪 Parent Coaching Focus: Scaffold and Step Back
🏠 Try This at Home

Make a tong transfer station at home — two bowls, a pair of kitchen tongs, and anything small: cotton balls, grapes, blocks. How many can you transfer? Count together. Then try playdough scooping: two containers, one small spoon. Which is harder — the tongs or the spoon?

🫀 Body Awareness This Week

At snack this week, coach parents explicitly: "No encouraging one more bite. If they stop — they're done. Your job was the what, when, and where. Their job was the whether and how much."

Communication Pod
Early Storytelling — Beginning, Middle, End
🦉 OWL
▾
Why This Matters

Narrative sequencing — the ability to tell a coherent story with beginning, middle, and end — is the single most powerful predictor of later reading comprehension. At 3, children can typically sequence three known events. The three-picture cards provide a scaffold for children not yet generating sequences independently. The puppet show models the structure the child will internalise.

🦉 OWL Reminder — Observe, Wait, Listen

OWL — Observe: when your child tells you something, notice whether there's a sequence. 'And then... and then...' is the beginning of narrative. Follow it before adding your own.

Activities
  • 3-picture story sequence (EYLF 5): 'First, then, then last.' Three pictures of a familiar event. Put them in order together. Tell the story.
  • Child describes play (EYLF 4): 'Tell me what happened with the blocks.' Accept and expand any attempt.
  • Puppet show with structure (EYLF 5): 'One day, puppet found a ball. Then puppet lost the ball. Then puppet found it again!' Clear beginning-middle-end.

VariationDraw and tell: child draws three simple pictures of what happened today, then tells the story from them.

👪 Parent Coaching Focus: Describe and Narrate
🏠 Try This at Home

After any event this week — a trip out, a bath, a meal — try: 'tell me what happened. What did we do first? What happened then? What happened at the end?' Accept any response.

📚 Book of the Week — Hanen Book Nook

The Tiger Who Came to Tea — Judith Kerr

Clear three-part narrative structure — matching the beginning, middle, end storytelling theme. After reading, try retelling together: "What happened first? Then what? How did it end?"

Cognitive Pod
Shape Awareness — Recognition, Sort, Hunt
EF: WM (hold shape template in mind)
▾
Why This Matters

Shape recognition is the gateway to spatial reasoning and to letter recognition — letters are combinations of shapes. The outdoor shape hunt is the most important activity this week because it requires cognitive flexibility: the child must apply the shape template (circle) to objects that are circles but don't look like 'a shape toy' — wheels, plates, manhole covers. This real-world generalisation is a critical cognitive step.

🧠 Executive Function Skill This Week

Working Memory (hold shape template in mind) + Cognitive Flexibility (find shapes in varied contexts)

Activities
  • Shape sorting board (EYLF 4): Circle, square, triangle. Model rotation of pieces before handing back.
  • Shape puzzles (EYLF 4): More complex shapes — star, heart, hexagon — build differentiation beyond the three primary shapes.
  • Outdoor shape hunt (EYLF 2): 'Find me a circle outside.' Windows, wheels, stepping stones, plates. What else is a circle?

VariationDraw shapes in sand or a tray — adds kinesthetic (tactile-motor) memory to visual recognition. Drawing shapes builds the neural pattern for letter formation.

👪 Parent Coaching Focus: Follow Child's Lead
🏠 Try This at Home

Go on a shape hunt in your home — find 3 circles, 3 squares, 3 rectangles. Photograph them if you can and make a 'shape book' to look at together.

Equipment
Shape sorting boardShape puzzlesChalk for outdoor shapesSand tray (variation)

Social-Emotional Pod
Responding to Name and Peer Recognition
CASEL: Social Awareness
▾
Why This Matters

By week 9, children should be able to name their peers in the group. Peer name recognition is a social milestone: the child understands that the other children in the group are known individuals, not interchangeable others. In a group setting, being named by peers and naming them in return is an experience of community that builds belonging and social identity.

Activities
  • Name call in group song (EYLF 5): Adapt a familiar song to include each child's name: 'Hello [name], how are you today?'
  • Hide & seek with name calls (EYLF 5): 'Where's [name]?' Child emerges or calls back.
  • Clap name syllables (EYLF 5): Clap the syllables of each child's name. Builds phonological awareness alongside social recognition.

VariationPhoto name cards — each child's photo on a card. Children find their own card, then find others' cards.

🌱 Development Note (Erikson)

Social identity: 'I am [name], and I am part of this group.' This is the earliest social identity formation beyond the family.

👪 Parent Coaching Focus: Warm Attention — Group Belonging

Parents understand that peer name recognition is a developmental milestone and a social identity marker — being named and naming others is how a 3-year-old begins to experience themselves as a member of a community.

🏠 Try This at Home

Use your child's name in songs at home: 'Old MacDonald had a farm — and [name] was there!' They love it. And ask about the other children by name: 'what was [child] doing today?'

Equipment
Name song (adapted)Photo name cards

Self-Help Pod
Carrying and Delivering — IADL Helper Roles
ADL: IADL
▾
Why This Matters

Carrying and delivering tasks are the earliest form of social contribution — doing something useful for others, not just for oneself. The specificity matters: carry this item to that person creates a clear purposeful goal with a social outcome. This is qualitatively different from 'put the toy away' because it involves another person.

Activities
  • Carry toy to shelf (EYLF 3): One toy from the floor to the designated shelf. Child chooses which toy.
  • Deliver block to peer (EYLF 2): 'Can you take this block to [name]?' Named recipient, specific item.
  • Carry snack cup from prep area (EYLF 3): Child carries their own cup from where snack is prepared to their seat.

VariationGive the child a small tray — making the delivery role feel official and important.

⚠️ Common Parent Trap

Parents give 'pretend helper' tasks while doing the real task themselves. Only real tasks with real social consequences build genuine industry.

🌱 Development Note (Erikson)

Industry: the 3-year-old who delivers something to a specific person in a specific place is experiencing purposeful agency in a social context. This is the Eriksonian industry task in miniature.

👪 Parent Coaching Focus: Support Then Release
🏠 Try This at Home

Give your child one real delivery job each day — carry the napkins to the table, bring a toy to a sibling, take their cup to the sink. Named person, specific item, real outcome.

Small tray for deliveringDesignated shelf at child height
