Week 5

Week 5 extends body part labelling to function vocabulary in communication, introduces controlled running as the locomotion FMS focus, and moves cognitive sorting into three-category groupings. Social-emotional play transitions from parallel to associative — children beginning to reference each other's activity. Self-help targets socks and shoes with celebration of independence.

Gross Motor Pod
Locomotion: Running with Control and Direction Change
FMS: Running
▾
Why This Matters

At 3, running with control includes direction change — a more advanced skill than simply stopping. Changing direction while running requires spatial awareness, motor planning, and balance. 'Red Light, Green Light' builds inhibitory control alongside locomotion. Munch & Move (2018): running cues for 3–5 year olds include arm opposition — left arm forward with right leg, right arm forward with left leg — beginning to emerge.

🏃 FMS Technique Cues (Munch & Move)
Eyes straight ahead
Swing your arms in opposition to legs
Knees up
Change direction without slowing down (advanced)
Run to the cone — freeze at the line
Activities
  • Follow-the-leader (EYLF 3): Adult leads a running path with deliberate direction changes, stops, and speeds. Children copy.
  • Red Light Green Light (EYLF 3): Green = run, red = freeze. Add 'yellow = walk slowly' for a three-level challenge.
  • Chasing bubbles (EYLF 3): Directional running with a natural target — pop the bubble. Encourages acceleration and spatial awareness.

VariationAdd obstacle cones to weave between — builds agility and direction-change skill.

👪 Parent Coaching Focus: Scaffold and Step Back
🏠 Try This at Home

Play Red Light Green Light at home — green = run, yellow = walk, red = freeze. Add 'silly walk!' for fun. The stopping and the direction changes are the skill.

Cones for weaving pathDrum or music for signalsBubble machine or solution

Communication Pod
Body Part Labelling with Function Vocabulary
🦉 OWL
▾
Why This Matters

At 3, body part vocabulary extends beyond naming to function: not just 'hands' but 'hands for holding, building, eating.' This function vocabulary is the bridge between concrete labelling and early conceptual understanding. It also builds the vocabulary children need to communicate their own needs and describe their bodies.

🦉 OWL Reminder — Observe, Wait, Listen

OWL — Observe: when your child touches or uses a body part, name it and add its function. 'Hands — you're using your hands to build!'

Activities
  • 'Head, Shoulders' with function (EYLF 5): After naming each part, ask 'what do we do with our...?' Prompt: 'ears for hearing, eyes for seeing, hands for...'
  • Mirror — name and function (EYLF 3): Child points to their own body part in the mirror, names it, and demonstrates its function.
  • Body part picture matching (EYLF 4): Match picture cards of body parts to a full-body poster — then describe the function.

VariationExtend to: 'what do we do with our knees/elbows/fingers?' — moves into increasingly specific function vocabulary.

👪 Parent Coaching Focus: Describe and Narrate
🏠 Try This at Home

Sing 'Head, Shoulders' in the bath — and after naming each part, add its job: 'nose — and our nose is for smelling!' Let your child fill in the function.

Equipment
MirrorBody part picture cardsFull-body poster or outline

Cognitive Pod
Three-Category Sorting
EF: CF (three-way rule)
▾
Why This Matters

Three-way sorting is a significant step up from two-way: it requires holding three rules simultaneously and applying each to multiple objects. At 3–3.5 years, this is developmentally challenging. Harvard (2014) and Blakey et al. (2015): cognitive flexibility in sorting tasks develops rapidly at exactly this age — this week catches the window.

🧠 Executive Function Skill This Week

Cognitive Flexibility (three-way rule) + Working Memory (hold multiple category rules)

Activities
  • Three colour sort (EYLF 4): Three containers, three colours. Provide objects of all three colours — sort them together.
  • Animals/cars/food (EYLF 4): Three clear categories, mixed objects. Name the category before each placement.
  • 'Why do these go together?' (EYLF 5): After sorting, ask why — introduce reasoning language: 'because they're all animals,' 'because they're all red.'

VariationAfter completing the three-way sort — switch the rule: 'now let's sort them by big and small instead.' Three-way → two-way switch. The flexibility challenge.

👪 Parent Coaching Focus: Follow Child's Lead
🏠 Try This at Home

Sort the toy box into three groups together — animals, vehicles, figures. Ask 'why do these go together?' after each group. Try switching the rule: 'now let's sort by colour.'

Equipment
Mixed colour toysAnimal, vehicle, food play setsThree sorting containers

Social-Emotional Pod
Parallel to Associative Play Transition
CASEL: Relationship Skills
▾
Why This Matters

At 3, parallel play is giving way to associative play — children beginning to reference, comment on, and adjust to peers' activity without yet having shared goals. The facilitator and parent role is to notice and narrate these moments: 'he looked at what you were building!' Narration makes the implicit social event explicit and memorable.

Activities
  • Blocks side-by-side (EYLF 2): Same materials in a shared space. Watch for the first moments of referencing — child looks at what a peer is doing. Narrate immediately.
  • Bubble play (EYLF 1): Shared space, shared experience. Natural peer interaction around catching and popping bubbles.
  • Sandpit with peers (EYLF 2): Open-ended shared space. Adult narrates: 'Mia is digging over here — you're both digging!'

VariationAdult models associative play language: 'I'm building a tower. What are you building?' — gives children a script for the social move.

🌱 Development Note (Erikson)

Initiative in social play: the first moment a child adjusts their play because they've noticed a peer is a significant social initiative. Celebrate it when it happens.

👪 Parent Coaching Focus: Narrate the Social World

Parents learn to observe and narrate peer interactions rather than engineering them. 'He's watching what you're building' is more useful than 'say hello to Josh.'

🏠 Try This at Home

Arrange a playdate with one other child. Don't organise activities — just provide the same materials and watch. Narrate what you see: 'you're both building!'

Equipment
Large block setsBubble equipmentSand/water play tray

Self-Help Pod
Shoes and Socks — Dressing Sequence
ADL: Dressing
▾
Why This Matters

At 3, the sock-off step should be fully independent — the appropriate start of foot dressing. Putting shoes on is the next target: starting with slip-ons, then velcro. The 'shoe helper' peer role builds social function into self-help practice.

Activities
  • Pull socks off independently (EYLF 3): Loose sock starting position — child pulls off. If still needing support, assess fine motor baseline.
  • Dress-up slip-on shoes (EYLF 1): Easy slip-on shoes or costume footwear — repeated practice in play context.
  • Celebrate with stickers or claps (EYLF 1): 'You did it yourself!' Name what happened specifically.

VariationCreate a 'shoe helper' role — child helps a peer manage their shoes. Social + self-help integration.

⚠️ Common Parent Trap

Shoes are the last step before going out — everyone is in a hurry. Reframe: practise shoe management at a non-urgent time first, then transfer to the morning routine.

🌱 Development Note (Erikson)

Initiative: the 3-year-old who takes off their own shoes and socks and then reverses it is demonstrating directional sequencing in self-help — the beginning of the 'I can manage my own body' narrative.

👪 Parent Coaching Focus: Support Then Release
🏠 Try This at Home

Shoes and socks off before bath every night this week — independently. Then try shoes on again before they get in the bath (for practice!). Celebrate every independent attempt.

Equipment
Velcro shoes and slip-onsLoose socks for practiceStickers for celebrating
