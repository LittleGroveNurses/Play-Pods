Gross Motor Pod
Fine Motor: Controlled Scribbling and Pre-Writing Marks
FMS: Grip Transition + Mark-Making Intentionality
▾
Why This Matters

Fine motor development at 3 years targets the grip transition from fist to tripod and mark-making intentionality — making specific directed marks rather than random scribbling. Horizontal and vertical lines are the pre-writing shapes that precede letter formation. At 3, circular scribble is also emerging — this is the precursor to the letter 'O' and to drawing faces.

🏃 FMS Technique Cues (Munch & Move)
Hold the crayon with three fingers (tripod grip emerging)
Whole arm for large marks — wrist for smaller ones
Horizontal line — pull across
Vertical line — push down
Circular scribble — keep going around
Activities
  • Crayons (EYLF 4): Large crayons on unlined A3 paper. Demonstrate horizontal line ('pull across'), vertical line ('push down'), circle ('keep going around'). Ask for each.
  • Sidewalk chalk (EYLF 4): Outdoor mark-making with whole-body engagement. Encourages larger arm movements before fine wrist work.
  • Chunky markers (EYLF 4): Felt-tip markers on large paper — lower resistance than crayons, good for hand strength at this stage.

VariationLarge brushes and paint — broad deliberate strokes on large paper. Process over product.

👪 Parent Coaching Focus: Scaffold and Step Back
🏠 Try This at Home

Leave paper and crayons on a low table. Let your child scribble whenever they choose. Ask 'can you draw me a line?' and 'can you make it go around?' rather than asking what they drew.

Equipment
Large crayonsChunky markersLarge paper (A3 or bigger)Sidewalk chalk

Communication Pod
Naming Actions — Verb Vocabulary
🦉 OWL
▾
Why This Matters

Action words (verbs) are the engine of sentence building. Children with strong verb vocabulary move into multi-word combinations faster than those with predominantly noun vocabulary. At 3, verb vocabulary should be growing rapidly — common verbs like eat, sleep, run, jump, fall, push, pull, and more abstract ones like hide, give, help.

🦉 OWL Reminder — Observe, Wait, Listen

OWL — Watch: before labelling what your child is doing, watch them for 3 seconds. Then name the action as they do it — not before.

Activities
  • Lotto action cards (EYLF 5): Cards showing actions. Child matches card to action or names the card.
  • Act out action words (EYLF 3): Adult says action word — child acts it out. Then swap — child says, adult acts. 'Jump!' 'Sleep!' 'Eat!'
  • Pretend doll play — narrate actions (EYLF 5): 'Baby is sleeping. Baby is eating. Baby is crying. Now baby is running!'

VariationFamily action photographs — 'what is Daddy doing? What is Grandma doing?' Higher personal relevance.

👪 Parent Coaching Focus: Describe and Narrate
🏠 Try This at Home

Narrate your own actions throughout the day: 'I'm cutting, stirring, pouring, driving, folding.' Every verb you say out loud is a verb your child is filing away.

Equipment
Action lotto cardsDoll for pretend playFamily action photographs (optional)

Cognitive Pod
Pretend Play — Complex Roles and Scripts + The Silence Game
EF: IC (stay in role; silence game — resist all sound and movement)
▾
Why This Matters

Harvard (2014): during intentional imaginary play, children develop rules to guide their actions in playing roles. They hold complex ideas in mind and shape their actions to follow these rules, inhibiting impulses that don't fit the role. At 3, pretend play extends from simple imitation to role-based play with a basic script — the doctor examines, the patient is sick. This is among the strongest EF-building activities at this developmental stage.

The Silence Game (Montessori, Control of Movement): a brief structured period of complete quiet and stillness — no talking, no moving — is one of the most direct inhibitory control exercises available to 3-year-olds. Unlike pretend play where IC is embedded in a story, the Silence Game makes IC the explicit, named challenge. Children find it surprisingly compelling once they understand what they're trying to do. It also functions as a powerful transition tool between pods.

🧠 Executive Function Skill This Week

Inhibitory Control (stay in role; silence game — resist all sound and movement) + Working Memory (hold role's rules and script)

Activities
  • Doctor/patient role play (EYLF 1): Doctor's kit — examine the patient, give a shot, write a prescription. Multiple roles, basic script.
  • Cooking and serving pretend (EYLF 5): Kitchen props. Cook, plate, serve, eat, compliment. Full sequence with multiple roles.
  • Shop and customer (EYLF 5): Items on a shelf, money, bag. 'What would you like? That's two dollars. Thank you, goodbye!'
  • The Silence Game (Montessori Control of Movement — IC): Signal the start by dimming lights or ringing a small bell. 'Let's see if we can all be completely quiet and still — no talking, no moving, no sounds — for as long as we can.' Start with 20–30 seconds. Celebrate together at the end: 'We did it — that was so hard and you all did it!' Extend to 60 seconds across later weeks.

VariationIntroduce props that support role clarity — a doctor's coat, an apron, a shopping bag. Costume elements deepen role commitment. The Silence Game can be used as a transition cue at any point in the session — not just in the cognitive pod.

🪜 Scaffolding This Activity

Pretend play: follow the child's lead entirely within the role structure. If the narrative goes somewhere unexpected — follow it. The script is a starting point, not a rule.

Silence Game: model it first yourself — sit very still, close your eyes, put your finger to your lips. Let children see what it looks like. Then invite them to try. Never enforce it — make it a game, not a demand. Any child who manages 5 seconds is succeeding.

🎯 Facilitator Coaching Cues

When a parent breaks the pretend frame: "Stay in the scene — you're the patient! He's working out how to be the doctor."

During the Silence Game: coach parents to participate rather than watch. 'You do it too — model what complete stillness looks like.' Parents who join in make it feel like a shared challenge rather than a child task.

👪 Parent Coaching Focus: Follow Child's Lead + Model the Challenge

Parents learn that their own participation in the Silence Game — genuinely trying to be still and quiet — is the most powerful model they can offer. And in pretend play, their role is to stay inside the story, not direct it.

🏠 Try This at Home

Try the Silence Game before bed — 'can we all be completely quiet for 30 seconds?' Use it as a settling ritual. Children who find it funny at first usually become quite invested in it within a week. And leave the prop box accessible for pretend play — observe what happens.

Equipment
Doctor's kitPretend kitchen propsShopping items and play moneyRole costumes/propsSmall bell or light dimmer for Silence Game signal

Social-Emotional Pod
Comfort-Seeking and Self-Regulation Strategies
CASEL: Self-Management
▾
Why This Matters

Self-regulation at 3 years is transitioning from predominantly co-regulation (needing an adult alongside) to the first stirrings of self-regulation strategies. The calm corner is a physical anchor. Slow breathing is one of the few physiologically effective self-regulation strategies appropriate for this age. At 3, introducing the breathing technique — even imperfectly — begins building the skill.

Activities
  • Hug soft toy (EYLF 1): Model: 'when I feel upset I hug teddy.' Have soft toys in the calm corner and accessible throughout the session.
  • Calm corner (EYLF 3): Quiet area with soft items, books, sensory objects. Introduce it proactively — not only when a child is dysregulated.
  • Slow breathing — 'smell the flowers, blow the candles' (EYLF 3): Child sniffs in through the nose (smelling flowers), blows out through the mouth (blowing candles). Simple, memorable, physiologically effective.

VariationAdd a simple 3-face feelings chart to the calm corner. Child points to how they feel when entering.

🎯 Facilitator Coaching Cues

When a parent escalates alongside the dysregulated child: "Can we take a breath together — literally? The calmer you are, the faster he'll regulate. Your nervous system is the co-regulation tool."

When a child is dysregulated: "This is the moment — not to teach, not to explain, just to be present. 'I can see you're really upset. I'm here.' That's it."

🌱 Development Note (Erikson)

Initiative through safety: the 3-year-old who can use the calm corner independently — even once — is demonstrating self-initiated self-regulation. This is initiative directed inward.

👪 Parent Coaching Focus: Co-Regulate First

Parents learn that their own regulated state is the most powerful self-regulation intervention available to their child. You cannot teach a dysregulated child — you can only be with them.

🏠 Try This at Home

Set up a small calm corner at home — a cushion, soft toy, and one or two books. Practise the 'smell the flowers, blow the candles' breathing together when you're both calm. Then it's there when needed.

Equipment
Soft toys and blanketsCalm corner setupSimple feelings chart

Self-Help Pod
Snack Preparation and Routine Helper Roles
ADL: IADL
▾
Why This Matters

At 3, snack preparation is the richest self-help activity available — it integrates fine motor skill (peeling, scooping, pouring), sequencing (step-by-step preparation), and genuine real-world contribution. The Montessori Practical Life framework identifies food preparation as a core Care of Self and Care for the Environment activity for toddlers and preschoolers. A child who prepares their own snack is not just practising a skill — they are developing a narrative about who they are: 'I am someone who can do real things.'

Critically, this week elevates the IADL framework from carrying and delivering (Week 9) to actual production — the child makes something that didn't exist before they started. This is qualitatively different from tidying, and is one of the most motivating self-help experiences available at this age.

Activities
  • Peel and slice banana (EYLF 3): Provide a ripe banana and a child-safe spreading knife or banana slicer. Show once, slowly. 'Peel from the top. Now slice — one piece at a time.' Step back entirely. The child prepares their own snack.
  • Scoop cereal or dried fruit into bowl (EYLF 3): Small jug or container of cereal. Child scoops into their own bowl using a large spoon. Count the scoops together.
  • Carry own snack plate to table (EYLF 3): Child carries the plate they prepared from the preparation area to their seat at the table. The whole sequence is theirs.
  • Pack one toy away (EYLF 3): Single-item cleanup. Name the toy as it goes in. 'The red car goes in the box.'

VariationGive the child a 'helper badge' — even a sticker makes the role feel officially real. For children ready for more: orange squeezing using a child-sized juicer is a next-level food preparation challenge that 3-year-olds find deeply satisfying.

🪜 Scaffolding This Activity

Banana peeling: show the sequence once with minimal words — hold at the top, peel down, place peel in the bowl. Then hand it to the child. Don't demonstrate again — let them work it out.

Scooping: place the spoon in the child's hand, position the bowl, then step back. If the cereal spills — that's fine. 'Oops — shall we scoop it up?'

Carrying: ensure the plate is not overloaded. Child carries at their own pace. No rushing.

🎯 Facilitator Coaching Cues

When a parent peels the banana for the child: "Let's give him the banana and see what he does — 3-year-olds can often do more than we expect when we step back."

When a parent worries about mess: "The mess is part of the process — it means she's working independently. Let's put a mat down and let her go."

When a child succeeds: "She prepared her own snack. Name that specifically: '[Child's name] made her own snack today — she peeled it, sliced it, carried it. That is real work.'"

⚠️ Common Parent Trap

Parents often pre-prepare snack 'to save time' and then give the child a pretend preparation task. The message: the real preparation IS the activity. A banana prepared by the child and eaten by the child is more developmentally valuable than 10 minutes of any other self-help practice. Set aside 5 extra minutes and let it be real.

🌱 Development Note (Erikson)

Industry: the 3-year-old who prepares their own snack — peels a banana, scoops their cereal, carries it to the table — is not helping. They are working. This is the Eriksonian industry task in its most authentic early childhood form.

👪 Parent Coaching Focus: Real Work — Not Pretend Help

Parents understand the difference between giving a child a pretend task and giving them real work with real consequences. This week is about making the distinction clear and concrete through snack preparation.

🏠 Try This at Home

Try the banana preparation at home this week — peel, slice, carry to the table. Or let your child scoop their own cereal into their bowl. Set aside the extra few minutes. The mess is manageable. What they're building is not.
