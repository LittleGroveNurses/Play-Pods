Week 2

Week 2 emphasises vocabulary expansion and naming skills while providing opportunities for climbing, crawling, and balancing through structured motor play. Cognitive activities encourage early problem-solving with puzzles and nesting cups, while social-emotional learning focuses on separation comfort and smooth transitions. Self-help routines continue building independence in drinking and snack routines.

Gross Motor Pod
Stability: Climbing and Step Work
FMS: Climbing
▾
Why This Matters

At 3 years, alternating feet on stairs is the developmental target (unlike the marking-time pattern appropriate for 2-year-olds). This is a significant motor milestone that requires planning, weight transfer, and spatial awareness. The climbing frame and obstacle course build upper body strength, coordination, and risk assessment in a supported environment.

🏃 FMS Technique Cues (Munch & Move)
One hand on rail or wall for support
Lead foot steps up first — alternating feet is the goal
Eyes look ahead not down
Knees bend on descent
Choose your own route up — don't be directed
Activities
  • Climbing frame (EYLF 3): Safe climbing structure with multiple paths. 3-year-olds can begin to plan their route up and down — give them time to do this.
  • Step climbing (EYLF 3): Practice stepping up and down stairs with alternating feet. Handrail available but not always needed.
  • Crawl + climb obstacle course (EYLF 3): Tunnels, cushions, and steps in sequence — builds motor planning and sequencing.
  • Body awareness (EYLF 3): 'Touch your knees and jump' — games that pair naming body parts with movement.

VariationTape footprints showing alternating foot pattern on steps — visual guide for developing the pattern.

👪 Parent Coaching Focus: Scaffold and Step Back
🏠 Try This at Home

Find three steps at home or the park. Walk up and down slowly, alternating feet: left, right, left, right. Count the steps out loud. Can they do it without holding on?

🫀 Body Awareness This Week

When your child chooses the harder route — or the easier one — that's them reading their body's readiness. Name it: "You knew your body was ready for that one." Trusting the body's own assessment is the foundation of interoception.

Equipment
Climbing frame or playground stepsCushions and tunnels for obstacle courseFootprint tape (variation)

Communication Pod
Vocabulary Expansion — Naming with Descriptors
🦉 OWL
▾
Why This Matters

The key language step this week is adding descriptive words to labels: not just 'ball' but 'big red ball.' Children with vocabulary delays benefit from hearing the same object described in multiple ways across multiple contexts. The outdoor walk embeds vocabulary in the real world — the most durable learning context.

🦉 OWL Reminder — Observe, Wait, Listen

OWL — this week focus on the Wait. When your child reaches for something or looks at something, count silently to five before you label it. They may have the word already.

Activities
  • Name 5 toys with descriptors (EYLF 5): Hold up toys one at a time. Wait. When the child labels, model adding a descriptor: 'Yes, big truck!'
  • Mystery bag (EYLF 5): Cloth bag with familiar objects. Child reaches in, feels, describes, then pulls out. 'Is it soft? Round? Big?'
  • Outdoor naming walk (EYLF 2): Walk the yard or street naming things. Child leads — adult follows and labels what the child notices. Add adjectives: 'big tree,' 'yellow flower.'

VariationPhotograph objects on the walk and make a simple naming book to revisit each week.

👪 Parent Coaching Focus: Observe and Follow
🏠 Try This at Home

Try the mystery bag at home — put a familiar object in a pillowcase. 'Is it soft? Is it round? Big or small?' Wait for any response before looking.

📚 Book of the Week — Hanen Book Nook

Elmer — David McKee

Rich descriptive vocabulary — colours, patterns, feelings — matching the naming with descriptors theme. Pause at each descriptive moment: "How would you describe Elmer?" Model adding a descriptor to whatever word your child offers.

Problem-Solving — Cause and Effect Tools
EF: WM (hold goal in mind)
▾
Why This Matters

Problem-solving at 3 years builds the 'if I do X, Y happens' mental model — the basis of both scientific reasoning and social prediction. Harvard (2014): when a child holds a goal in mind and adjusts their approach after failure, they are practising working memory and inhibitory control simultaneously. The key developmental shift from 2 to 3 is increasing tolerance for trying multiple approaches before giving up.

🧠 Executive Function Skill This Week

Working Memory (hold goal in mind) + Inhibitory Control (try again differently when first attempt fails)

Activities
  • Pull-string toy (EYLF 4): Toy on a string just out of reach. Child must work out to pull the string. Don't demonstrate — let them discover.
  • Knob puzzles (EYLF 4): Wooden puzzles with single-piece lift-out shapes. At 3, expect the child to rotate pieces and attempt placement with more intention than at 2.
  • Nesting cups (EYLF 4): Stack and nest in size order. Introduce 'which comes first — big or small?' language while stacking.

VariationAdd 'which comes first?' and 'what happens next?' language — building narrative prediction alongside physical problem-solving.

👪 Parent Coaching Focus: Follow Child's Lead
🏠 Try This at Home

Nesting cups in the bath — fill them, pour them, stack them. Ask 'which cup is biggest? Which is smallest? Which goes inside which?' Let your child figure it out.

Equipment
Pull-string toyWooden knob puzzles (shapes or animals)Nesting cups (5–8)

Social-Emotional Pod
Separation and Transitions
CASEL: Self-Management
▾
Why This Matters

Separation distress is common in the early weeks of a group program, especially for 3-year-olds with developmental vulnerability who may have had fewer group experiences. The goal is not to eliminate distress — it is to give children the emotional language and predictable routine to manage it. Gottman (1997): naming the emotion is more effective than reassurance because it validates the child's experience.

Activities
  • Consistent short goodbye (EYLF 1): Same words, same duration, same warmth every time. Coach parents to commit to this before the session.
  • Comfort toy (EYLF 3): Allow children to bring or choose a comfort object. It is a co-regulation tool, not a crutch.
  • Transition song (EYLF 5): Use a consistent song to signal each transition within the session. Predictability across the session reduces the anxiety of any single transition.

VariationEncourage peer reassurance — children learn the song together, making the transition a shared group experience.

🎯 Facilitator Coaching Cues

When a parent prolongs the goodbye: "The kindest thing — and the hardest — is a warm, brief goodbye. The longer it stretches, the harder it gets for both of them. Can we try a 10-second version?"

When a child is distressed at transition: "She's telling us she knows you're going — that's secure attachment, not separation anxiety. Let's name it: 'you feel sad. Mummy is coming back after lunch.'"

🌱 Development Note (Erikson)

Initiative: the 3-year-old who can manage a separation with a named emotion and a comfort object is building the emotional courage to take social initiative. Security precedes initiative.

👪 Parent Coaching Focus: Name It to Tame It

Parents learn that naming the child's emotion — 'you feel sad' — is more helpful than rushing to fix it or dismissing it with 'you'll be fine.'

🏠 Try This at Home

Practise a goodbye routine at home before the next session — same words, same hug, same wave every time. By week 4 or 5, it usually becomes much smoother.

Equipment
Comfort toys from homeTransition song (recorded or live)

Drinking — Open Cup and Pouring
ADL: Feeding
▾
Why This Matters

By 3, children should be managing an open cup reliably. The new skill this week is pouring from a small pitcher — a fine motor and coordination challenge that also builds the sense of agency and contribution.

Activities
  • Practice open cup (EYLF 3): Small cups with a small amount of liquid. Stabilise the table surface, not the cup.
  • Tea party pouring pretend (EYLF 1): Small pitcher to cups in play context. Playful, low-pressure, high repetition.
  • Self-pour snack cup (EYLF 3): Child pours their own drink at snack from a child-sized jug.

VariationUse real fruit water or herbal tea — sensory engagement increases motivation and enjoyment.

⚠️ Common Parent Trap

Parents intervene when pouring is about to spill. The message: place a towel under the cup, accept the spill, and let the child experience the consequence — 'oops, it spilled. Let's pour more slowly.' The spill is the lesson.

🌱 Development Note (Erikson)

Initiative: the 3-year-old who pours their own drink is demonstrating purposeful action in the world — 'I can make things happen.'

👪 Parent Coaching Focus: Support Then Release
🏠 Try This at Home

Let your child pour their own drink at one meal a day. Use a small amount in a small jug. Put a towel down. Let it spill — that's the practice.
