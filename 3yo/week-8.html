Week 8

Gross Motor Pod
Locomotion: Galloping
FMS: Galloping
▾
Why This Matters

Galloping is a locomotor skill that emerges around age 3 and is often the first asymmetrical locomotion pattern (one foot consistently leads). Munch & Move (2018): 'step forward with one foot — second foot pushes the front foot in the air — toe to heel.' The clip-clop rhythm is the key — children who find the rhythm find the movement. The fun horse theme supports sustained engagement.

🏃 FMS Technique Cues (Munch & Move)
Step forward with one foot — the lead foot
Second foot pushes the front foot in the air
Toe to heel rhythm — listen for the clip-clop
Arms slightly bent — help the rhythm
Same foot leads throughout
Activities
  • Free galloping (EYLF 3): Open space, music, free galloping. Demonstrate the clip-clop rhythm with a drum or hands.
  • Galloping relay (EYLF 3): Gallop to the cone and back, tag the next person. Simple relay format with team waiting.
  • Red Light Green Light — gallop version (EYLF 3): Gallop on green, freeze on red.

VariationMove Like a Horse — pictures of horses in a bowl, children 'become' the horse and gallop with theme. Build the narrative.

👪 Parent Coaching Focus: Scaffold and Step Back
🏠 Try This at Home

Gallop together at the park or down the hallway — show the clip-clop rhythm with a drum on your leg or hands. 'Same foot leads! Clip-clop, clip-clop!'

Equipment
Open spaceDrum for rhythmHorse pictures (variation)

Communication Pod
Using Pronouns — I, You, He, She
🦉 OWL
▾
Why This Matters

Pronoun use requires the child to understand that the same person is referred to differently depending on perspective — a complex cognitive-linguistic task. Errors like 'me want' or 'him running' are developmentally normal at 3 and should be modelled correctly without correction. The key is consistent correct modelling in natural contexts.

🦉 OWL Reminder — Observe, Wait, Listen

OWL — Listen: hear whether your child is starting to use 'me,' 'mine,' 'I.' When you hear them, reflect them back: 'yes, yours!'

Activities
  • Role-play at snack (EYLF 5): 'I'm eating. You're eating. He's eating.' Model in real time during snack.
  • Story he/she (EYLF 5): Read books with clear character identities and use he/she consistently when referring to them.
  • Imitation 'me/you' (EYLF 5): Touch your chest: 'me.' Touch child's chest: 'you.' Touch another child's chest: 'her!' Make it a playful pointing game.

VariationUse family photos — 'what is she doing? What is he doing?' Higher personal relevance than book characters.

👪 Parent Coaching Focus: Describe and Narrate
🏠 Try This at Home

Use pronouns explicitly at meals: 'I have a spoon. You have a fork. He has a cup.' Don't correct when they get confused — just model correctly in your next sentence.

Equipment
Picture books with clear character pronounsFamily photos

Cognitive Pod
Sequencing — Steps, Stacks, and Patterns
EF: WM (hold sequence in mind)
▾
Why This Matters

Sequencing at 3 years is building the cognitive skill that underlies routine competence, narrative comprehension, early maths, and eventually reading. Harvard (2014): imitation games requiring children to remember and copy a sequence practice attention, working memory, and self-control simultaneously. The key 3-year-old extension is 3–4 element sequences (2-element is solidly within 2-year-old range).

🧠 Executive Function Skill This Week

Working Memory (hold sequence in mind) + Inhibitory Control (complete each step before rushing to next)

Activities
  • Block tower in steps (EYLF 4): Adult places blocks one at a time with deliberate pauses. Child copies the full sequence.
  • Ring stack in size order (EYLF 4): Largest to smallest. Scaffold only if completely stuck — the working-out is the learning.
  • 3–4 clap sequence (EYLF 5): Adult claps a pattern of 3 or 4 elements. Child copies. Introduce rhythm variation (slow-slow-fast).

Variation3-picture sequence cards — 'wake up → get dressed → eat breakfast.' Which comes first?

👪 Parent Coaching Focus: Follow Child's Lead
🏠 Try This at Home

Draw 3 pictures of the morning routine together. Each night before bed, put them in order: 'what do we do first tomorrow?' The routine is the sequence.

Equipment
Unit blocksSize-ordered stacking ringsClap sequence rhythmSequence picture cards

Social-Emotional Pod
Turn-Taking — Extended and Partner Games
CASEL: Self-Management
▾
Why This Matters

At 3, turn-taking extends from adult-child dyads to peer-peer dyads and small group turns. The developmental target is extending the wait tolerance — 3-year-olds can typically wait for 2–3 turns before their own. Naming the wait explicitly ('you're waiting — that's the hardest part!') validates the effort and builds self-monitoring.

Activities
  • Roll ball back and forth — count turns (EYLF 1): Count the turns out loud. See how high you can get. Celebrate 5 turns as a milestone.
  • Pass drum around circle (EYLF 1): Each child has the drum for a count of four, then passes. Names the receiver: 'now [name]'s turn.'
  • Simple board game (EYLF 5): Simple turn-based game (snakes and ladders, lotto). Models the turn structure in a play context.

VariationVisual turn token — a bright card that physically shows whose turn it is. Reduces disputes because the object is the authority.

🌱 Development Note (Erikson)

Initiative in social play: the 3-year-old who waits for their turn and then acts — instead of grabbing — is demonstrating social initiative: choosing to engage within the rules of the interaction.

👪 Parent Coaching Focus: Narrate the Wait

Parents learn that narrating the wait — 'you're waiting so well!' — is more effective than redirecting when a child struggles.

🏠 Try This at Home

Roll a ball back and forth at home — count the turns out loud. See how many you can get to. 5 in a row is a genuinely great achievement for a 3-year-old.

Equipment
Soft ballDrumSimple board gameTurn token card

Self-Help Pod
Self-Feeding — Fork, Spoon, and Cup Independence
ADL: Feeding
▾
Why This Matters

By 3, the goal is a full meal — using fork, spoon, and cup — with minimal parent support. The parent's role is to set up the environment for success (appropriate utensils, appropriately sized pieces) and then step back entirely. Celebration language should describe the specific achievement, not just praise.

Activities
  • Full meal with fork and spoon (EYLF 3): Set up the meal and step back. Observe what the child can do without support.
  • Open cup without spilling (EYLF 3): Small amount in the cup — increases chance of success. Stabilise the table surface.
  • Celebrate independence (EYLF 1): 'You ate your whole lunch yourself!' Name what happened. Avoid 'good boy/girl' — use specific description.

VariationChopstick helpers for interested 3-year-olds — high challenge, high engagement.

⚠️ Common Parent Trap

Parents set up the meal and then hover, helping before the child struggles. Reframe: set up, step back, and only intervene if asked or if struggle is genuinely distressing.

🌱 Development Note (Erikson)

Initiative at the table: the 3-year-old who feeds themselves a full meal with minimal support is demonstrating the self-directed action that characterises this stage. Food is one of the earliest and most frequent arenas for asserting autonomy and initiative.

👪 Parent Coaching Focus: Step Back and Observe
🏠 Try This at Home

This week, set up one meal with appropriately sized food and the right utensils — then sit beside your child and do nothing unless asked. Notice what they can actually do. You might be surprised.

Equipment
Age-appropriate utensilsSmall cupsAppropriately sized food pieces
