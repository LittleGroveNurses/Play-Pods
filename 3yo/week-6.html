Gross Motor Pod
Stability: Balance — Static and Dynamic
FMS: Static and Dynamic Balance
▾
Why This Matters

Balance is the stability FMS that underpins all locomotion and object control skills. Static balance (standing still, one-footed) and dynamic balance (moving along a beam) have different neural demands. Diamond (2000): balance tasks require sustained focused attention and continuous body monitoring — directly building EF alongside the motor skill.

🏃 FMS Technique Cues (Munch & Move)
Eyes fixed on a still point ahead
Arms out for balance — like wings
Soft knees — never locked
One foot at a time on the beam
Hold the frozen pose when music stops
Activities
  • Walk low beam or tape line (EYLF 3): Low balance beam or line of tape on the floor. Eyes ahead, arms out. Heel-to-toe progression.
  • Wobble cushion standing (EYLF 3): Stand on a cushion or pillow. Challenges proprioceptive system.
  • Freeze in balance pose to music (EYLF 3): Music plays — dance or move. Music stops — hold a balance pose. Builds static balance and inhibitory control.

VariationCarry a beanbag on the head while walking the beam — adds an attentional balance challenge.

👪 Parent Coaching Focus: Scaffold and Step Back
🏠 Try This at Home

Walk a line of tape on the floor heel-to-toe. Arms out for balance. See how far you can go without stepping off. Take turns — who can go furthest without stepping off the line?

🫀 Body Awareness This Week

Balance requires your body to feel where it is in space — that's proprioception. Try asking your child to close their eyes and balance on one foot: "Your body knows where it is even without looking — it's listening to itself."

Communication Pod
Using Plurals
🦉 OWL
▾
Why This Matters

Plural morphology (adding -s to nouns) is a grammatical milestone that emerges through consistent modelling in context. At 3, children are typically at the stage of acquiring regular plurals — they may be using some but not applying the rule consistently. Consistent adult modelling of plurals in natural contexts is more effective than drilling.

🦉 OWL Reminder — Observe, Wait, Listen

OWL — Observe: watch whether your child is noticing when there's more than one of something. They may already be using plurals — listen for them.

Activities
  • Show 2 toys — 'cars!' (EYLF 5): Hold up two identical toys, name them as a group: 'cars! Two cars!'
  • Farm play with multiple animals (EYLF 5): 'The cows are eating. Three horses!'
  • Story with plural nouns (EYLF 5): Choose books deliberately featuring plural nouns — 'the ducks, the trees, the children.'

VariationDeliberately misuse singular: 'look at the duck...' (when there are clearly three). See if the child corrects you — they often do, and it means the rule is forming.

👪 Parent Coaching Focus: Describe and Narrate
🏠 Try This at Home

Point out plurals as you go through the day: 'shoes! two shoes. cups! three cups.' You don't need to drill it — just model it naturally and consistently.

Cognitive Pod
Memory — Sequential and Location
EF: WM (sequential memory
▾
Why This Matters

Two distinct working memory systems are being exercised this week: visuospatial working memory (location of hidden toy) and sequential verbal working memory (clapping pattern). Harvard (2014): hiding games exercise working memory as the child mentally tracks the object's location. At 3, the clapping sequence can extend to 3–4 elements, building the phonological loop that directly supports language acquisition.

🧠 Executive Function Skill This Week

Working Memory (sequential memory + location tracking)

Activities
  • Hide toy, same spot (EYLF 4): Hide in the same location 3 times, then move it. Does the child check the old spot first? This is the A-not-B error — normalising it reduces parent concern.
  • Clap pattern copy (EYLF 5): Two-clap pattern — child copies. Then 3, then 4. Celebrate when they get it. Model celebrating getting it wrong too.
  • Routine song recall (EYLF 5): Songs with repeated verses — Old MacDonald, This Old Man. Build the sequential memory through music.

VariationSpinning turntable — hide object under cup, spin the turntable. Adds tracking challenge to the location memory task.

👪 Parent Coaching Focus: Follow Child's Lead
🏠 Try This at Home

Play 'clap copy' at dinner — you clap a pattern, they copy. Start with 2 claps. Build up to 4. It's harder than it sounds. Take turns being the pattern maker.

Equipment
Small toys for hidingTurntable (optional)Drum for clapping

Social-Emotional Pod
Stranger Awareness and New Adults
CASEL: Self-Management
▾
Why This Matters

Wariness of unfamiliar adults at 3 is developmentally appropriate and healthy — it reflects secure attachment and good discernment. The goal is not to eliminate the wariness but to give children the emotional language, the safe base, and the graduated exposure to manage it. The group song is ideal for introducing a new adult because it is social contact mediated by a shared activity rather than direct interaction.

Activities
  • Warm introduction (EYLF 1): Name the new adult, describe their role, allow observation from the parent's arms.
  • Familiar toy present (EYLF 1): Comfort object visible and accessible throughout the encounter.
  • Group song including new adult (EYLF 5): New adult participates in the song alongside children — indirect social contact.

VariationHave the new adult bring an interesting object to share — children approach objects before they approach people.

🎯 Facilitator Coaching Cues

When a parent pushes their child toward a new adult: "Let's give him a minute to watch from your arms — he's working out whether it's safe. You can say 'that's the nurse, she's kind' and let him take his time."

When a child refuses: "She's showing us healthy discernment — she uses her safe base to assess. That's exactly what we want her doing."

🌱 Development Note (Erikson)

Initiative: the child who, from a secure base, eventually takes the initiative to approach a new adult is demonstrating exactly the social courage this stage requires.

👪 Parent Coaching Focus: Safe Base

Parents understand that their calm, available presence — not pushing — is the scaffold for stranger regulation. Their job is to be the base the child launches from when ready.

🏠 Try This at Home

When your child meets a new person this week, stay close, name the person calmly, and let your child approach in their own time. Resist the urge to push. Narrate what you both observe.

Equipment
Comfort objects accessibleNew adult briefed on warm-but-non-intrusive approach

Self-Help Pod
Brushing Teeth — Full Routine
ADL: Hygiene
▾
Why This Matters

At 3 years, the teeth brushing goal shifts from compliance to routine ownership. The child who brushes first — even for 10 seconds — before the adult finishes, is building the habit through participation. The song provides duration and structure. The mirror provides immediate feedback.

Activities
  • Doll tooth brushing (EYLF 5): Child brushes doll's teeth while adult sings the brushing song. Builds the schema before the real thing.
  • Real brushing — child first (EYLF 3): Child brushes for their song's duration, adult finishes. Never skip the child's turn.
  • Mirror practice (EYLF 3): Watch in mirror while brushing. Seeing the action motivates participation and builds self-monitoring.

VariationLet the child choose their toothbrush from two options — control over the object increases ownership and cooperation.

⚠️ Common Parent Trap

Parents do the full brush themselves to ensure it's done properly. Reframe: an imperfect child-led brush followed by a parent finish is better than parent-only brushing for building the routine — and for building the child's sense of ownership of their own oral care.

🌱 Development Note (Erikson)

Initiative: at 3, the child who asks for their toothbrush — or goes to find it — is exercising purposeful self-directed behaviour. This is the beginning of a lifelong hygiene habit.

👪 Parent Coaching Focus: Support Then Release
🏠 Try This at Home

Child brushes first every night — even for 10 seconds. Same song every time. You finish after. By the end of the week, notice whether your child starts picking up the toothbrush before you suggest it.
