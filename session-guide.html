Session Guide

A consistent, evidence-based structure for every Play Pods session across all age groups. The structure is fixed; the content within each phase is drawn from the week's session manual.

Session at a Glance — ~60 minutes total

🌟 Phase 1 — Welcome and Arrival

This phase sets the emotional tone for the entire session. The facilitator greets every child by name at child eye level. The arrival undressing routine is one of the most valuable self-help practice moments of the session.

Three-Step Cueing Approach

  1. Set up — open the shoe, pull the sock to the starting position
  2. Cue — single verbal prompt: 'Push!' / 'Pull!' / 'Arms through!'
  3. Wait — count silently to 5. Watch. Resist doing it for them.
⚠️ Common Pitfall

Parents rush through undressing to get into play quickly. Reframe: 'The 2 minutes on shoes is the self-help practice for the session.'

🎵 Phase 2 — Opening Songs

Opening songs signal that the session has begun. Always start with the Hello Song (name-based) — same words, same melody, every week. Choose 1–2 additional songs that link to this week's session skills (see Song Reference below).

🦉 OWL Moments in Songs

Coach parents to use the expectant pause during songs:

  • Old MacDonald: pause before the animal sound — wait 5 seconds
  • Head Shoulders: drop a word — 'did she notice the missing word?'
  • Twinkle Twinkle: pause mid-phrase — wait for completion
🪀 Phase 3 — Play Pod Rotation 1 (Two Pods)

Suggested default: Motor Pod + Communication Pod. These are the highest-energy and most structured pods — run them while children are freshest. The facilitator moves between pods, spending approximately 8–10 minutes in each.

🎯 Your Role in the Pod

The facilitator's primary task is coaching the parent, not directing the child. If you find yourself doing an activity with a child while the parent watches — stop. Hand it back to the parent.

'The parent is the intervention. The facilitator is the coach of the parent.'

🍎 Phase 4 — Snack Time

Snack time is not a break. It is a structured practice opportunity for self-help skills and a prime parent coaching window. Sit at the table with families.

Reflective Questions — Choose One Per Session

  • 'What did you notice about [child's name] today — anything that surprised you?'
  • 'Was there a moment in the pod where you wanted to help but you waited?'
  • 'Which skill felt easiest to step back on? Which was hardest?'
  • 'What's one thing you'll try at home this week?'
🪜 Snack Preparation (3-Year-Olds)

Consider having children prepare their own snack — peel a banana, scoop cereal. The preparation is the practice. It takes 3 extra minutes and is worth every second.

🪀 Phase 5 — Play Pod Rotation 2 (One Pod)

One pod after snack — usually Cognitive, Social-Emotional, or Self-Help. Lower energy post-snack makes this ideal for quieter, more focused play. Often the deepest parent coaching window of the session.

🧠 The Silence Game (3-Year-Old Groups)

Dim the lights or ring a small bell: 'Let's see if we can all be completely quiet and still for 30 seconds.' An effective transition tool that simultaneously builds inhibitory control. The facilitator participates alongside the children. Start with 20–30 seconds; extend to 60 seconds over later weeks.

🎵 Phase 6 — Closing Songs and Farewell

The closing ritual mirrors the opening. Always end with the Goodbye Song. After songs, transition immediately to the departure dressing routine — the second self-help coaching window of the session.

👪 Departure Dressing

Use exactly the same three-step cueing approach as arrival. By weeks 5–7, many children will begin to self-initiate dressing at departure.

After dressing: farewell to each child and parent individually. Hand each family the take-home handout: 'Here's one thing to try at home this week.'

🎵 Song Reference

Select 1–2 songs per session that link to the week's session skills. Always include the Hello Song (opening) and Goodbye Song (closing) as fixed ritual anchors.

SongAgePod / SkillWhy It Works
Hello Song (name-based) All ages SE — Belonging Fixed ritual anchor. Peer name recognition, turn-taking, group belonging signal every week.
Hop Little Bunnies 2–3 years Motor: Hopping + IC Hopping FMS with structured stop/go cue. Freeze phase builds inhibitory control.
Five Little Speckled Frogs 2–3 years Motor: Jumping + WM Countdown builds working memory and number sequencing alongside jumping FMS.
Grand Old Duke of York 2–4 years Motor: Running + IC Direction change (up/down) embedded in lyrics — inhibitory control through movement.
Freeze Dance 2–4 years Motor: Locomotion + IC Any locomotion during play; freeze on signal. Primary EF target: inhibitory control.
Old MacDonald (with pause) 1–2 years Communication: OWL Pause before animal sound — 5 second wait. Classic OWL expectant pause at single-word level.
Head Shoulders Knees and Toes 2–4 years Communication + IC + WM Body part vocabulary. Drop a word each round: IC (resist saying it) + WM (hold which is missing).
BINGO (letter drop) 3–4 years Communication: WM Progressive cloze — letter replaced by clap. Phonological working memory. Best for 3–4 year olds.
Boom Chicka Boom 2–4 years SE: Turn-taking Call-and-response models social reciprocity. Facilitator leads, group echoes.
If You're Happy (extended) 2–4 years SE: Emotion naming Extend with sad, angry, scared, surprised verses. Emotion naming + body actions.
Ring Around the Rosie 1–3 years SE: Group cooperation Shared physical experience, proprioceptive regulation, shared IC moment at 'all fall down'.
Goodbye Song All ages SE — Transition ritual Mirror of Hello Song. Fixed ritual. Teaches children that endings are safe.
